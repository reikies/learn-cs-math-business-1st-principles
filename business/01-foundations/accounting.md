# Accounting & Financial Literacy — The Language of Business

> If you can't read a balance sheet, you can't run a business. Accounting is not
> about number-crunching — it's about understanding the story of where money came
> from, where it went, and what's left. It is the language in which every business
> conversation is ultimately conducted.

**Prerequisites:** Basic arithmetic. Conceptually, [Economics](economics.md) helps you understand *why* these numbers matter.
**What it enables:** [Corporate Finance](../05-finance/corporate-finance.md), [Financial Modeling](../05-finance/financial-modeling.md), fundraising, every financial decision.

---

## Why This Matters

Warren Buffett calls accounting "the language of business." He's not being poetic — he's being literal.

Every investor, lender, partner, and acquirer evaluates your business by reading your financial statements. Every board meeting, every fundraising pitch, every acquisition negotiation comes down to the numbers. If you don't understand them, someone else controls your story.

You don't need to become an accountant. You need to become fluent — able to read, interpret, and challenge the numbers that define your business.

---

## 1. The Accounting Equation

Everything in accounting rests on one equation:

```
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

This is not optional. It is not approximate. It **always** balances. Every single transaction, no matter how complex, maintains this equation.

**Assets** — What the company *owns* (cash, inventory, equipment, patents, accounts receivable)
**Liabilities** — What the company *owes* (loans, accounts payable, deferred revenue, bonds)
**Equity** — What's left for the owners after paying all debts (initial investment + retained earnings)

Think of it this way: everything your company has (assets) was funded by either borrowing (liabilities) or investment/profits (equity).

---

## 2. Double-Entry Bookkeeping

The 600-year-old system that makes accounting work. Every transaction affects at least two accounts, keeping the equation in balance.

Example: You buy a laptop for $1,500 cash.

| Account | Type | Change |
|---------|------|--------|
| Cash | Asset | -$1,500 |
| Equipment | Asset | +$1,500 |

Assets still equal liabilities + equity. The total didn't change — just the composition.

Example: You take a $50,000 bank loan.

| Account | Type | Change |
|---------|------|--------|
| Cash | Asset | +$50,000 |
| Bank loan | Liability | +$50,000 |

Both sides increase by the same amount. Balance maintained.

Example: You earn $10,000 in revenue.

| Account | Type | Change |
|---------|------|--------|
| Cash | Asset | +$10,000 |
| Revenue → Equity | Equity | +$10,000 |

Revenue increases equity (retained earnings). This is how profitable businesses grow their equity over time.

## 3. The Three Financial Statements

These three documents tell the complete financial story of any business. They are interconnected — each one feeds into the others.

### The Income Statement (Profit & Loss / P&L)

**Question it answers:** Did the company make or lose money over a period of time?

```
Revenue (what you earned)                          $500,000
  - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)                     -$200,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Gross Profit                                     $300,000
  - Operating Expenses (salaries, rent, marketing) -$220,000
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Operating Income (EBIT)                           $80,000
  - Interest expense                                -$5,000
  - Taxes                                          -$18,750
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
= Net Income (bottom line)                          $56,250
```

Key concepts:
- **Revenue** (top line) — total money earned from selling products/services
- **COGS** — direct costs of producing what you sell (materials, manufacturing, hosting)
- **Gross margin** = Gross Profit / Revenue (here: 60%) — how much you keep after direct costs
- **Operating expenses (OpEx)** — costs of running the business, not directly tied to production
- **EBITDA** — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization — a popular measure of operating performance
- **Net income** (bottom line) — what's actually left. Flows into retained earnings on the balance sheet.

### The Balance Sheet

**Question it answers:** What does the company own, owe, and what's left for owners at a single point in time?

```
ASSETS                              LIABILITIES
─────────────                       ─────────────
Current Assets                      Current Liabilities
  Cash           $100,000             Accounts Payable    $30,000
  Accounts Rec.   $50,000             Short-term Debt     $20,000
  Inventory       $40,000
                                    Long-term Liabilities
Non-Current Assets                    Bank Loan          $100,000
  Equipment      $200,000
  Patents         $80,000           EQUITY
                                    ─────────────
                                      Owner's Investment  $250,000
                                      Retained Earnings    $70,000

Total Assets     $470,000           Total L + E          $470,000
```

Key concepts:
- **Current** = converts to/from cash within one year
- **Accounts receivable** = money customers owe you (you delivered, they haven't paid yet)
- **Accounts payable** = money you owe suppliers
- **Retained earnings** = accumulated net income that hasn't been distributed to owners
- **Book value** = total equity. What's theoretically left if you sold everything and paid all debts.

### The Cash Flow Statement

**Question it answers:** Where did cash actually come from and go to?

This is the most important statement for a startup. You can be "profitable" on the income statement and still run out of cash and die.

```
Cash from Operating Activities
  Net Income                          $56,250
  + Depreciation (non-cash expense)   $20,000
  - Increase in Accounts Receivable  -$15,000
  + Increase in Accounts Payable      $10,000
  ─────────────────────────────────
  Net Operating Cash Flow             $71,250

Cash from Investing Activities
  - Equipment purchase               -$50,000
  ─────────────────────────────────
  Net Investing Cash Flow            -$50,000

Cash from Financing Activities
  + New bank loan                     $30,000
  - Loan repayment                   -$10,000
  ─────────────────────────────────
  Net Financing Cash Flow             $20,000

NET CHANGE IN CASH                    $41,250
```

**The critical insight: profit ≠ cash.**

You can be profitable and bankrupt simultaneously. How?
- Your customer owes you $500,000 (revenue recognized, income statement looks great)
- But they haven't paid yet (accounts receivable — cash hasn't arrived)
- Your rent is due tomorrow

This is why "cash is king." Revenue is an opinion. Cash is a fact.

## 4. Accrual vs. Cash Accounting

Two ways to record transactions:

**Cash accounting:** Record revenue when cash arrives. Record expenses when cash leaves. Simple, but misleading for anything beyond a lemonade stand.

**Accrual accounting:** Record revenue when it's *earned* (product delivered, service rendered) and expenses when they're *incurred* — regardless of when cash moves.

Example: In December, you deliver $50,000 of consulting services. The client pays in February.

| Method | December | February |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Cash | $0 revenue | $50,000 revenue |
| Accrual | $50,000 revenue | $0 revenue |

Accrual accounting gives a more accurate picture of economic reality. It's required by GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) for any serious business. But it also means you must watch cash flow separately — the income statement alone won't tell you if you can make payroll.

## 5. Key Financial Ratios

Ratios let you compare companies of different sizes and answer specific questions:

### Profitability

| Ratio | Formula | What it tells you |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Gross margin | Gross Profit / Revenue | How much you keep after direct costs |
| Operating margin | Operating Income / Revenue | How efficiently you run the business |
| Net margin | Net Income / Revenue | What % of revenue becomes profit |
| ROE | Net Income / Equity | Return generated on owner's investment |

### Liquidity (can you pay your bills?)

| Ratio | Formula | What it tells you |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Current ratio | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | Can you cover short-term debts? (>1 = yes) |
| Quick ratio | (Cash + Receivables) / Current Liabilities | Same, but without counting inventory |
| Burn rate | Monthly cash outflow | How fast you're spending (startups) |
| Runway | Cash / Monthly Burn Rate | How many months until you're dead (startups) |

### Leverage (how much debt?)

| Ratio | Formula | What it tells you |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Debt-to-equity | Total Debt / Equity | How leveraged is the business? |
| Interest coverage | EBIT / Interest Expense | Can you afford your debt payments? |

### SaaS-specific metrics

| Metric | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| MRR / ARR | Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue — the engine |
| Churn rate | % of customers lost per period |
| LTV | Lifetime Value — total revenue from an average customer |
| CAC | Customer Acquisition Cost — what you spend to get one customer |
| LTV/CAC | Should be > 3x for a healthy SaaS business |
| Payback period | Months to recoup CAC |

## 6. Revenue Recognition

One of the trickiest areas in accounting. **When** do you count revenue?

The rule (simplified): Revenue is recognized when the **performance obligation** is satisfied — meaning you've delivered the product or service.

| Scenario | When to recognize |
|----------|-------------------|
| Sell a widget, deliver immediately | At delivery |
| Annual SaaS subscription paid upfront | Ratably over 12 months |
| Consulting project, 50% upfront deposit | As work is performed |
| Gift cards sold | When the gift card is redeemed |

Why this matters for startups: If someone pays you $120,000 upfront for an annual subscription, your **cash** increased by $120,000 today. But your **revenue** only increases by $10,000 per month. The remaining $110,000 sits on your balance sheet as **deferred revenue** (a liability — you owe the customer 11 more months of service).

This is one of the main reasons why profit ≠ cash.

## 7. Depreciation and Amortization

When you buy a $100,000 machine that lasts 10 years, you don't expense the full $100,000 in year one. You spread the cost over its useful life: $10,000/year for 10 years. This is **depreciation** (for physical assets) or **amortization** (for intangible assets like patents).

Why it matters:
- It reduces reported profits each year (lowering taxes)
- It's a **non-cash expense** — you already paid for the machine. The depreciation is just bookkeeping.
- This is why cash flow statements add depreciation back to net income

### EBITDA

Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. By stripping out these items, EBITDA attempts to show the "pure operating performance" of a business. It's widely used in valuations but also widely criticized — Charlie Munger calls it "bullshit earnings" because it ignores real costs like capital expenditure.

## 8. Taxes (The Essentials)

You don't need to be a tax expert, but you need to know the basics:

- **Corporate income tax** — applied to net income (profits), not revenue
- **Sales tax / VAT** — collected from customers, passed through to the government. Not your money.
- **Payroll taxes** — the employer's share of Social Security, Medicare, etc.
- **Capital gains tax** — tax on profit from selling assets (including equity)

Key concept: **Tax deductions** reduce your taxable income. Expenses like salaries, rent, and depreciation are deductible. This is why companies reinvest profits — paying for things the business needs is better than paying taxes on idle cash.

## 9. Common Accounting Pitfalls

### Confusing revenue with cash
Your P&L says you made $1M in revenue. Your bank account has $50,000. Welcome to accounts receivable.

### Ignoring unit economics
You sell each unit for $10 with a $12 cost. "We'll make it up in volume!" No, you won't. You'll lose money faster.

### Forgetting about working capital
You need to buy inventory *before* you sell it. If your business grows 2x, your inventory needs grow 2x too — and that cash goes out before revenue comes in.

### Treating funding as revenue
Raising $5M in venture capital is not $5M in revenue. It's a liability (to your investors) in exchange for equity. Your income statement doesn't change. Your balance sheet does.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Assets = Liabilities + Equity.** Always. No exceptions.
2. **Three statements tell the whole story.** Income (did you profit?), Balance Sheet (what do you have?), Cash Flow (can you survive?).
3. **Profit ≠ cash.** Companies die from running out of cash, not from running out of profit.
4. **Accrual accounting is the standard.** Revenue is recognized when earned, not when cash arrives.
5. **Ratios let you compare and diagnose.** Gross margin, burn rate, LTV/CAC — know your key metrics.
6. **Revenue recognition is tricky.** Especially with subscriptions, prepayments, and long-term contracts.
