# Economics — How the World Allocates Scarce Resources

> Economics is the study of how people make choices when they can't have everything.
> Every business operates inside an economy — a system of billions of interconnected
> decisions about what to produce, how to produce it, and who gets it. If you don't
> understand the water you're swimming in, you'll drown.

**Prerequisites:** None. This is where it all begins.
**What it enables:** Strategy, pricing, market analysis, fundraising — every business decision.

---

## Why This Matters

Every business is an economic actor. You set prices, hire workers, compete for customers, and respond to regulation. These aren't random activities — they follow patterns that economists have studied for centuries.

You don't need a PhD in economics to build a business. But you need to understand a handful of core ideas deeply, because they explain *why* markets behave the way they do, *why* some pricing strategies work and others don't, and *why* competition grinds most profits to zero.

This isn't theory for theory's sake. This is the operating system your business runs on.

## 1. Scarcity and Trade-offs

The foundational insight of all economics:

**Resources are limited. Wants are unlimited. Therefore, every choice has a cost.**

That cost isn't just money. It's the **opportunity cost** — the value of the next-best thing you gave up.

If you spend $100,000 hiring an engineer, the cost isn't just $100,000. It's whatever else you could have done with that money — another engineer, a marketing campaign, six more months of runway.

Opportunity cost is the single most important concept in business decision-making. Every "yes" is a "no" to something else.

### The production possibilities frontier

Imagine a startup that can allocate its engineers to either Feature A or Feature B, but not both fully:

```
Feature B
│
│  *  (all on B)
│   \
│    \
│     \  ← Production Possibilities Frontier
│      \
│       \
│        *  (all on A)
└─────────────── Feature A
```

Points on the curve = efficient (you're using all your capacity).
Points inside the curve = wasteful (you could do more).
Points outside the curve = impossible with current resources.

This applies to everything: time, capital, attention, engineers. The frontier forces you to choose.

## 2. Supply and Demand

The most powerful model in economics, and the one you'll use every day in business.

### Demand: what buyers want

**The law of demand:** When the price of something goes up, people buy less of it. When the price goes down, people buy more.

Why? Because at higher prices, fewer people find it worth the cost, and those who do buy less.

A demand curve shows the relationship:

```
Price ($)
│
│  \
│   \
│    \    Demand curve
│     \
│      \
│       \
└─────────── Quantity
```

But price isn't the only thing that shifts demand. These factors shift the entire curve:

- **Income** — people with more money buy more (usually)
- **Substitutes** — if a competitor's product gets cheaper, your demand drops
- **Complements** — if the price of hot dogs drops, demand for hot dog buns rises
- **Preferences** — trends, reviews, word-of-mouth
- **Expectations** — if people expect a price drop, they wait

### Supply: what sellers offer

**The law of supply:** When the price goes up, producers supply more (it's more profitable). When the price goes down, producers supply less.

```
Price ($)
│              /
│            /
│          /    Supply curve
│        /
│      /
│    /
└─────────── Quantity
```

### Equilibrium: where supply meets demand

The market price settles where the quantity buyers want equals the quantity sellers offer:

```
Price ($)
│        S /
│        / 
│    ───X───  ← Equilibrium (market-clearing price)
│      / \
│    /    D
│  /      
└─────────── Quantity
```

At the equilibrium price, there's no surplus and no shortage. This isn't a law of nature — it's a tendency. Real markets are always adjusting toward equilibrium, rarely sitting exactly on it.

### Why this matters for your business

- **Price too high?** You'll have unsold inventory (surplus).
- **Price too low?** You'll have more demand than you can serve (shortage) — and you're leaving money on the table.
- **Competitor enters your market?** Supply shifts right → equilibrium price drops → your margins shrink.
- **You create a new market?** You're defining both the supply and the demand curve.

## 3. Elasticity — How Sensitive Is Demand?

Not all products respond to price changes the same way. **Elasticity** measures sensitivity.

**Price elasticity of demand** = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price

| Elasticity | What it means | Examples |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Elastic (> 1) | Demand is very sensitive to price | Luxury goods, restaurants, airline upgrades |
| Inelastic (< 1) | Demand barely changes with price | Insulin, gasoline, electricity |
| Unit elastic (= 1) | % change in price = % change in quantity | Rare in practice |

### Why this matters for pricing

If your product has **inelastic** demand, you can raise prices without losing many customers. This is the dream — it means you have pricing power.

If your product has **elastic** demand, raising prices costs you a lot of customers. You compete on volume and efficiency.

What makes demand inelastic?
- **No close substitutes** (insulin, not aspirin)
- **It's a necessity** (electricity, not concert tickets)
- **Small share of budget** (salt, not a car)
- **Switching costs** (enterprise software you've built workflows around)

Building a business with inelastic demand is essentially building a moat (see [Competitive Strategy](../02-strategy/competitive-strategy.md)).

## 4. Markets and Competition

### The spectrum of competition

Markets aren't all the same. They range from cutthroat to monopolistic:

| Structure | Players | Pricing power | Examples | Profit outlook |
|-----------|---------|--------------|----------|---------------|
| Perfect competition | Many | None — price taker | Commodity farming, forex | Minimal long-run profit |
| Monopolistic competition | Many | Some — differentiation | Restaurants, clothing brands | Moderate, eroded over time |
| Oligopoly | Few | Significant — interdependent | Airlines, telecom, auto | Can be high, depends on coordination |
| Monopoly | One | Maximum | Utilities (regulated), Google Search | High, unless regulated |

### The brutal truth about competition

In a perfectly competitive market, **economic profit trends toward zero** over time. If you're making above-normal profits, new entrants flood in, supply increases, and prices drop until the profit disappears.

This is why strategy matters. The entire point of competitive strategy is to avoid perfect competition — to build barriers that keep your market from becoming a commodity.

Peter Thiel puts it bluntly: **"Competition is for losers."** Monopoly is the goal. Every business should try to be the *only* one doing what it does.

### Market failures

Markets don't always work perfectly. Key failures:

- **Externalities** — costs or benefits that hit people who aren't in the transaction (pollution, education). Businesses often ignore negative externalities unless regulated.
- **Information asymmetry** — one side knows more than the other (used car sales, insurance). This creates adverse selection and moral hazard.
- **Public goods** — things that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous (national defense, open-source software). Hard to fund through markets.
- **Market power** — monopolies can charge above competitive prices.

Understanding market failures helps you spot opportunities (solve an externality = create value) and risks (your market might get regulated).

## 5. Incentives — The Hidden Architecture

> "Show me the incentives and I will show you the outcome." — Charlie Munger

Incentives are the most underrated force in business. People respond to incentives — not to what you *want* them to do, but to what you *reward* them for doing.

### Incentive alignment

When someone's incentives align with yours, cooperation is natural:
- **Equity compensation** aligns employees with company success
- **Commission-based sales** aligns salespeople with revenue
- **Performance bonuses** align managers with targets

### Incentive misalignment

When incentives diverge, behavior gets weird — and predictably so:
- **Real estate agents** get paid on commission → they push for quick closes, not the best price for you
- **Doctors paid per procedure** → more procedures, whether needed or not
- **Employees paid by the hour** → no incentive to finish faster
- **VCs who need home runs** → they push portfolio companies toward risky bets, even when a solid single would be better for the founders

### The principal-agent problem

When one person (the **principal**) hires another (the **agent**) to act on their behalf, their incentives often diverge. This is everywhere in business:

- Shareholders (principal) vs. CEO (agent)
- Founder (principal) vs. hired manager (agent)
- Customer (principal) vs. financial advisor (agent)

Solutions: equity stakes, transparent metrics, reputation systems, skin in the game.

## 6. Marginal Thinking

Economists don't think in averages. They think at the **margin** — the cost or benefit of *one more unit*.

**Marginal cost:** What does it cost to produce one more unit?
**Marginal revenue:** What do you earn from selling one more unit?

The rule: **Keep producing as long as marginal revenue > marginal cost.** Stop when they're equal.

This is why:
- **Airlines sell last-minute seats cheap** — the marginal cost of one more passenger (a bag of pretzels, a bit of fuel) is near zero. Any revenue above that is profit.
- **Software has near-zero marginal cost** — once built, each additional user costs almost nothing. This is why software companies can scale to insane margins.
- **You should ignore sunk costs** — money already spent is irrelevant. Only the marginal cost of continuing vs. stopping matters.

### Sunk cost fallacy

The biggest violation of marginal thinking. "We've already spent $2M on this project, we can't stop now." Yes, you can. The $2M is gone regardless. The only question is whether the *next* dollar spent will produce more value than it costs.

Killing projects that aren't working is one of the hardest — and most important — things a founder does.

## 7. Game Theory — Strategic Interaction

When your outcome depends on what *other people* do, you're in a game. Game theory studies these situations.

### The Prisoner's Dilemma

The most famous game. Two prisoners, each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (betray):

|  | B cooperates | B defects |
|--|-------------|-----------|
| **A cooperates** | Both get 1 year | A gets 10, B goes free |
| **A defects** | A goes free, B gets 10 | Both get 5 years |

The rational move for each individual is to defect. But if both defect, they're worse off than if both cooperated. This is the core tension of competition: individually rational behavior can lead to collectively bad outcomes.

In business, this shows up as:
- **Price wars** — each company cuts prices, all margins evaporate
- **Advertising arms races** — each company spends more, none gains share
- **Overfishing / resource depletion** — each actor takes more, the commons collapses

### Nash Equilibrium

A **Nash equilibrium** is a state where no player can improve their outcome by changing their strategy alone. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, mutual defection is the Nash equilibrium — even though it's not the best outcome.

Understanding Nash equilibria helps you predict what competitors will do and design strategies that change the game.

### Repeated games

In one-shot games, defection wins. But business is a **repeated game** — you deal with the same customers, suppliers, and competitors over and over. In repeated games, cooperation becomes rational because:

- You can build **reputation** (and destroy it)
- You can **punish defection** in future rounds
- **Tit-for-tat** (cooperate first, then mirror what the other side does) is remarkably effective

This is why trust and reputation are so valuable in business. They're cooperative strategies in an infinitely repeated game.

## 8. Macro Forces That Shape Your Business

You don't operate in a vacuum. Macroeconomic forces affect every business:

### Interest rates
- **Low rates** → cheap to borrow → more startups, higher valuations, more risk-taking
- **High rates** → expensive to borrow → fewer startups, lower valuations, "focus on profitability"

### Inflation
- Prices rising across the economy. Your costs go up. You can either raise prices (risk losing customers) or eat the margin (risk losing your business).
- Some businesses benefit from inflation (those with real assets, pricing power).

### Economic cycles
- **Expansion** → hiring, growth, optimism, sometimes irrational exuberance
- **Recession** → layoffs, contraction, pessimism, sometimes great buying opportunities

The best founders don't try to predict cycles. They build businesses resilient enough to survive them.

### Currency and trade
- If you sell internationally, exchange rates matter.
- Trade policy (tariffs, sanctions) can upend supply chains overnight.

## 9. Behavioral Economics — When Humans Aren't Rational

Classical economics assumes rational actors. Behavioral economics shows that humans are predictably irrational:

| Bias | What it is | Business implication |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| Anchoring | First number heard dominates judgment | Show the expensive plan first |
| Loss aversion | Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains | "Don't miss out" > "You could gain" |
| Status quo bias | People stick with defaults | Make the profitable option the default |
| Endowment effect | People overvalue what they own | Free trials work because giving it up feels like a loss |
| Hyperbolic discounting | People heavily prefer now over later | Subscriptions beat one-time payments |
| Social proof | People follow the crowd | "10,000 customers" and testimonials drive conversion |
| Framing | How you present a choice changes the choice | "95% fat-free" vs. "5% fat" |

These biases aren't bugs — they're features of human cognition. Understanding them isn't manipulation. It's meeting your customers where they actually are, not where rational-actor models say they should be.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Every decision has an opportunity cost.** The cost of doing something is what you gave up.
2. **Supply and demand determine price.** Not your costs, not your wishes — the market.
3. **Elasticity determines pricing power.** Build products with inelastic demand.
4. **Competition erodes profits.** Avoid commodity markets. Build moats.
5. **Incentives drive behavior.** Align them carefully, or watch things go sideways.
6. **Think at the margin.** Ignore sunk costs. The only question is: what's the next dollar worth?
7. **Business is a repeated game.** Reputation and trust compound. Defection is expensive.
8. **People aren't rational.** Design for the human you have, not the one economists imagined.
