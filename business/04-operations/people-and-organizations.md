# People & Organizations — The Humans Who Make It Work

> Every business is, in the end, a group of people trying to accomplish something
> together. Technology, strategy, and capital are all amplifiers — but they amplify
> *people*. How you hire, organize, lead, and incentivize those people determines
> whether you build something extraordinary or watch talented individuals pull
> in different directions.

**Prerequisites:** [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md) — incentives and game theory. [Legal Foundations](../01-foundations/legal-foundations.md) — employment law. [Operations](operations.md) — the systems people operate within.
**What it enables:** Everything. No layer of business functions without people.

---

## Why This Matters

A mediocre team with a great idea will execute poorly. A great team with a mediocre idea will either pivot to something better or execute the mediocre idea so well that it works anyway.

Startups like to say "we're building a great team." But what does that actually mean? It means hiring the right people, organizing them effectively, giving them clear goals and autonomy, creating a culture that retains them, and leading through the inevitable crises.

This is the least formulaic part of business. There are no algorithms for managing humans. But there are principles that consistently separate good organizations from dysfunctional ones.

## 1. Hiring — The Highest-Leverage Activity

A single great hire changes a company's trajectory. A single bad hire poisons a team for months.

### What to look for

| Attribute | Why it matters | How to test |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Competence | Can they do the job? | Work sample tests, past results |
| Judgment | Can they make good decisions without being told? | Scenario questions, references |
| Drive | Will they push through when it gets hard? | Track record, evidence of initiative |
| Integrity | Can they be trusted? | References, consistency in stories |
| Culture add | Do they bring something the team needs? | Values alignment, team interviews |

### Hiring process design

```
Source → Screen → Interview → Work Sample → Reference Check → Offer
```

Each stage should filter for different things:
- **Resume screen** — baseline qualifications (fast, high volume)
- **Phone screen** — communication, motivation, basic fit (30 min)
- **Technical/functional interview** — can they do the work? (1-2 hours)
- **Work sample** — give them a real problem, see how they solve it
- **Culture/values interview** — are they someone the team wants to work with?
- **Reference checks** — talk to people who've worked with them. Ask specific questions.

### Common hiring mistakes

- **Hiring for credentials, not ability.** The best person for the job may not have the best resume.
- **Hiring too fast.** Pressure to fill seats leads to bad hires. A bad hire costs more than an empty seat.
- **Hiring too slow.** In a startup, empty seats mean work doesn't get done. Balance urgency with standards.
- **Hiring people like yourself.** Homogeneous teams have blind spots. Diverse perspectives catch mistakes.
- **Not selling the role.** Top candidates have options. You're interviewing them as much as they're interviewing you.

### The "bar raiser" concept (Amazon)

Every interview includes a "bar raiser" — someone from outside the hiring team whose job is to ensure the candidate raises the average quality of the company. If the bar raiser says no, it's a no.

## 2. Organizational Design — How You Structure the Work

### Functional vs. cross-functional

**Functional organization:** People grouped by skill (engineering, marketing, sales, finance).

```
CEO
├── VP Engineering
│   ├── Backend team
│   └── Frontend team
├── VP Marketing
│   ├── Content team
│   └── Growth team
├── VP Sales
└── VP Finance
```

- **Pros:** Deep expertise, clear career paths, efficient resource sharing
- **Cons:** Silos, slow cross-team coordination, "throw it over the wall" mentality

**Cross-functional organization:** People grouped by product or mission (small teams with engineering, design, product, etc.).

```
CEO
├── Team: Core Product
│   ├── Engineer, Engineer, Designer, PM
├── Team: Growth
│   ├── Engineer, Marketer, Analyst, PM
├── Team: Enterprise
│   ├── Engineer, Sales, PM
└── Shared Services (Finance, HR, Legal)
```

- **Pros:** Fast execution, ownership, autonomy, less coordination overhead
- **Cons:** Duplicated skills, harder to maintain technical consistency, career path complexity

Most startups start functional and shift to cross-functional as they grow. Most large companies are a hybrid.

### Span of control

How many people should one manager manage?

- **Narrow span** (3-5 direct reports) — more oversight, more management layers, slower communication
- **Wide span** (7-12 direct reports) — less oversight, flatter org, faster communication but managers are stretched thin

The right answer depends on the complexity of the work and the experience of the team. Senior, autonomous employees can handle wider spans. Junior employees or complex, interdependent work requires narrower spans.

### Conway's Law

> "Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure."

Your org chart determines your product architecture. If you have three teams, you'll build a three-part system. This isn't a bug — it's a law. Design your organization to match the system you want to build.

## 3. Culture — The Operating System of an Organization

Culture is not ping pong tables and free lunch. Culture is **the set of shared values and behaviors that determine how people act when no one is watching**.

### Culture is what you tolerate

What you permit defines your culture more than what you proclaim. If you say "we value honesty" but tolerate a top performer who lies, your real culture is "performance matters more than honesty."

### The Netflix culture framework

Netflix's famous culture deck identified key principles:

- **Freedom and responsibility** — give people maximum freedom, expect maximum responsibility
- **Context, not control** — share strategic context so people can make good decisions
- **Highly aligned, loosely coupled** — agree on goals, let teams figure out the how
- **Keeper test** — "If this person told me they were leaving, would I fight to keep them?" If not, give them a generous severance and find someone you would fight for.

### Psychological safety

Google's Project Aristotle found that the single most important factor in team performance is **psychological safety** — the belief that you won't be punished for speaking up, asking questions, or admitting mistakes.

Teams with high psychological safety:
- Surface problems early (before they become crises)
- Share information freely
- Take calculated risks
- Learn faster from failures

Teams without it:
- Hide mistakes
- Don't ask for help
- Avoid difficult conversations
- Optimize for looking good over being good

### Culture at scale

Culture is easy at 10 people — everyone absorbs it through osmosis. At 100 people, you need to be intentional. At 1,000, you need systems:

- **Hire for it** — culture fit (or culture add) should be an explicit evaluation criterion
- **Onboard into it** — new hire orientation should teach culture, not just processes
- **Reinforce it** — recognize and reward behavior that embodies the culture
- **Correct against it** — address behavior that violates the culture, regardless of performance

## 4. Management — Getting Work Done Through Others

### What management actually is

Management is not telling people what to do. It's creating the conditions for people to do their best work:

1. **Set clear goals** — people need to know what success looks like
2. **Remove obstacles** — what's preventing them from achieving the goals?
3. **Provide feedback** — regular, specific, actionable
4. **Develop people** — help them grow their skills and careers
5. **Make decisions** — when the team is stuck, decide and move forward

### One-on-ones

The single most important management practice. Weekly, 30 minutes, with each direct report.

**What to cover:**
- How are you doing? (personal, not just professional)
- What's going well?
- What's blocking you?
- How can I help?
- Career development and growth

**What to avoid:**
- Status updates (use async tools for that)
- Doing all the talking
- Canceling them regularly

### Goal-setting frameworks

#### OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

Popularized by Intel and Google.

- **Objective** — qualitative, aspirational, motivating. "Become the market leader in developer tools."
- **Key Results** — quantitative, measurable, time-bound. "Increase MAU from 50K to 200K." "Achieve NPS > 60." "Ship v2.0 by Q3."

OKRs work because they separate the *what* (objective) from the *how* (key results) and force measurability.

#### SMART Goals

Each goal should be:
- **S**pecific — not vague
- **M**easurable — you know when you've achieved it
- **A**chievable — stretch but not impossible
- **R**elevant — aligned with the company's priorities
- **T**ime-bound — has a deadline

### Delegation

The hardest transition for a new manager: doing less work yourself and getting more done through others.

**What to delegate:**
- Tasks others can do 80% as well as you (they'll learn the last 20%)
- Recurring tasks that don't need your judgment
- Tasks that develop the skills of the person receiving them

**What NOT to delegate:**
- Hiring and firing decisions
- Setting vision and strategy
- Performance reviews and sensitive conversations
- Tasks where the cost of error is catastrophic

## 5. Leadership — The Difference at Scale

Management is about execution. Leadership is about direction, vision, and inspiring people to follow.

### Management vs. Leadership

| Management | Leadership |
|-----------|-----------|
| Plans and budgets | Sets vision and direction |
| Organizes and staffs | Aligns people |
| Controls and solves problems | Motivates and inspires |
| Produces consistency | Produces change |
| Does things right | Does the right things |

You need both. A company with great leaders and no managers has inspiring vision and total chaos. A company with great managers and no leaders has excellent execution and no idea where it's going.

### Decision-making frameworks

#### One-way vs. two-way doors (Bezos)

- **One-way door** — irreversible or very expensive to reverse (selling the company, shutting down a product line). Decide carefully, involve many people.
- **Two-way door** — reversible. Can be undone if wrong (launching a feature, testing a pricing change). Decide quickly, don't overthink.

Most decisions are two-way doors. Treating them as one-way doors causes organizational paralysis.

#### Disagree and commit

When a team can't reach consensus, the leader decides, and everyone commits fully — including those who disagreed. The worst outcome is a team that half-commits because some members are still arguing.

### Communication at scale

As a company grows, the founder's ability to communicate directly with everyone disappears. Leadership communication must become:

- **Repetitive** — say the same thing 10 times. What's boring to you is new to someone.
- **Multi-channel** — all-hands, email, Slack, 1:1s. People absorb differently.
- **Simple** — compress your message to its essence. Complexity doesn't survive transmission through an organization.
- **Connected to "why"** — decisions without context breed cynicism and resistance.

## 6. Compensation and Incentives

How you pay people shapes how they behave. See [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md) for the theory. Here's the practice.

### Total compensation components

| Component | What it is | Who gets it |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Base salary | Fixed cash compensation | Everyone |
| Bonus | Variable cash, tied to performance | Most employees |
| Equity (options/RSUs) | Ownership stake in the company | Startups: usually everyone. Public: typically senior roles. |
| Benefits | Health insurance, 401k, PTO | Everyone |
| Perks | Meals, gym, learning budget | Varies |

### Equity compensation in startups

Early employees take lower salaries in exchange for equity — a bet on the company's future value.

| Join timing | Typical equity | Risk level | Potential upside |
|------------|---------------|------------|-----------------|
| Co-founder | 10-50% | Maximum | Maximum |
| First 5 employees | 0.5-2% | Very high | Very high |
| Employee 6-20 | 0.1-0.5% | High | High |
| Employee 21-100 | 0.01-0.1% | Moderate | Moderate |
| Post Series B | 0.001-0.01% | Lower | Lower |

### The incentive alignment problem

You get what you measure and reward:

| Incentive | Intended behavior | Unintended behavior |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Revenue targets | Sell more | Discount everything, chase bad customers |
| Lines of code | Write more code | Write verbose, unnecessary code |
| Tickets closed | Resolve issues fast | Close tickets without solving problems |
| Customer satisfaction | Delight customers | Over-promise, under-deliver to other teams |

Design incentives carefully. Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

## 7. Scaling the Team

### The stages of organizational growth

| Stage | Headcount | What changes |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Founding team | 1-5 | No managers. Everyone does everything. |
| Early team | 5-20 | First managers. First processes. Culture is explicit now. |
| Growth | 20-100 | Functional teams. Hiring managers become full-time job. Communication overhead grows. |
| Scale | 100-500 | Departments. Middle management. Need formal systems for everything. |
| Enterprise | 500+ | Multiple layers of management. Bureaucracy risk. Innovation slows. |

### The transition from founder to CEO

The hardest personal transition in business:

- At 10 people, you do the work
- At 50 people, you manage the people who do the work
- At 200 people, you manage the people who manage the people
- At 1000 people, you set direction and build culture

Each transition requires letting go of the previous level. Many founders can't make these transitions — and that's okay. Some step aside for a professional CEO. Others learn and grow with the company.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hiring is the highest-leverage activity.** One great hire or one bad hire changes everything.
2. **Org design shapes behavior.** Conway's Law means your org structure becomes your product architecture.
3. **Culture is what you tolerate.** It's defined by the worst behavior you permit, not the best values you proclaim.
4. **Management is enabling, not controlling.** Set goals, remove obstacles, give feedback, develop people.
5. **Leadership is about direction and alignment.** Especially as the company scales beyond direct communication.
6. **Incentives drive behavior.** Align compensation with the outcomes you actually want.
7. **Every stage requires a different organization.** What works at 10 people breaks at 100.
