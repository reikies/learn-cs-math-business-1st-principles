# Sales — Where Revenue Happens

> Marketing creates awareness. Product creates value. But sales is where money
> actually changes hands. It's the moment a potential customer says yes, signs the
> contract, and pays. Without sales, everything else is a cost center.

**Prerequisites:** [Product](product.md) — you need something to sell. [Marketing](marketing.md) — marketing fills the top of the funnel. [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md) — pricing and willingness to pay.
**What it enables:** Revenue, which enables everything. [Growth](../06-growth/growth-and-scaling.md), [Financial Modeling](../05-finance/financial-modeling.md).

---

## Why This Matters

Engineers often underestimate sales. "If we build a great product, it sells itself." This is almost never true.

Slack, Dropbox, and Figma all have bottom-up adoption — and they all have large sales teams closing six- and seven-figure enterprise deals. Even product-led companies eventually need sales.

Sales is not manipulation. It's the process of understanding a customer's problem, demonstrating that your product solves it, handling objections, and making it easy to buy. Done well, it creates value for both sides.

## 1. Sales Models — How You Sell

The right sales model depends on your price point and the complexity of your product.

### The sales model spectrum

```
Low price                                              High price
Low complexity                                    High complexity
High volume                                         Low volume
│                                                           │
▼                                                           ▼
Self-serve → Inside Sales → Field Sales → Strategic/Enterprise
($10-500/mo)  ($500-5K/mo)   ($5K-50K/mo)    ($50K+/year)
```

### Self-serve

Customer finds, evaluates, and buys without human interaction.

- Website → Free trial → Credit card → Customer
- **Examples:** Canva, Notion, most consumer SaaS
- **Strengths:** Low cost, scales infinitely, fast
- **Weaknesses:** Limited to simple products with clear value prop
- **Key metrics:** Conversion rate, time to first value

### Inside sales

Sales reps sell remotely (phone, video, email). No travel.

- Marketing lead → Demo → Trial → Negotiation → Close
- **Examples:** HubSpot (SMB), Salesforce (SMB segment)
- **Strengths:** Cost-effective, can handle moderate complexity
- **Weaknesses:** Hard to build deep relationships remotely
- **Key metrics:** Meetings booked, demo-to-close rate, average deal size

### Field sales

Sales reps meet customers in person. Enterprise deals.

- Multiple meetings → Technical evaluation → Security review → Legal → Procurement → Close
- **Examples:** Palantir, Oracle, large Salesforce deals
- **Strengths:** Builds trust for high-stakes purchases, navigates complex orgs
- **Weaknesses:** Expensive (travel, time, salaries), long cycles
- **Key metrics:** Pipeline value, win rate, sales cycle length

### Hybrid / PLG + Sales

Product-led growth creates initial adoption; sales expands it.

- User adopts free tier → Usage grows → Sales identifies expansion opportunity → Upgrades team/company to paid
- **Examples:** Slack, Zoom, Datadog
- **Strengths:** Low CAC acquisition + high ACV expansion
- **Key metrics:** Product-qualified leads (PQLs), expansion revenue

## 2. The Sales Funnel

Every sale follows a predictable journey:

```
Lead Generation
    │
    ▼
Lead Qualification (is this a real opportunity?)
    │
    ▼
Discovery (understand the customer's problem)
    │
    ▼
Demo / Presentation (show how you solve it)
    │
    ▼
Proposal / Pricing (formal offer)
    │
    ▼
Negotiation (handle objections, align on terms)
    │
    ▼
Close (signed contract, payment)
    │
    ▼
Onboarding → Expansion → Renewal
```

### Lead qualification: BANT

Not every lead is worth pursuing. BANT filters for quality:

- **B**udget — Can they afford it?
- **A**uthority — Are you talking to the decision-maker?
- **N**eed — Do they actually have the problem you solve?
- **T**imeline — Are they buying now or "someday"?

If any of these is missing, the deal is unlikely to close — or will take much longer than expected.

### MQL vs. SQL vs. PQL

| Type | Definition | Who owns it |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead) | Someone who engaged with marketing (downloaded whitepaper, attended webinar) | Marketing |
| SQL (Sales Qualified Lead) | Someone sales has spoken to and confirmed as a real opportunity | Sales |
| PQL (Product Qualified Lead) | Someone whose product usage signals buying intent (hit usage limits, invited team) | Product / Sales |

The handoff from MQL to SQL is one of the biggest friction points in any company. Marketing thinks sales doesn't follow up. Sales thinks marketing sends garbage leads. Alignment requires shared definitions and metrics.

## 3. Discovery — The Most Important Step

Most salespeople talk too much about their product. Great salespeople spend most of their time *listening*.

### The discovery call

The goal is to understand:

1. **What's the problem?** "Tell me about how you currently handle X."
2. **How painful is it?** "What happens when that goes wrong?"
3. **What have you tried?** "How have you attempted to solve this?"
4. **Who else cares?** "Who else in your organization is affected?"
5. **What does success look like?** "If you solved this, what changes?"
6. **What's the timeline?** "When do you need this solved by?"
7. **What's the budget?** "Have you allocated budget for this?"

### SPIN Selling

Neil Rackham's research-backed framework for complex sales:

- **S**ituation questions — understand their current state
- **P**roblem questions — uncover their pain points
- **I**mplication questions — explore the consequences of not solving the problem
- **N**eed-payoff questions — get them to articulate the value of solving it

The key insight: the best salespeople don't tell customers they have a problem. They ask questions that lead customers to *realize* they have a problem and articulate *why* it matters.

## 4. Pricing and Negotiation

### Pricing principles

- **Price on value, not cost.** If your product saves $1M/year, charging $200K is a deal.
- **Anchor high.** The first number sets the frame. It's easier to discount down than to raise up.
- **Tiered pricing captures more value.** Different customers have different willingness to pay.
- **Annual contracts beat monthly.** Lower churn, upfront cash, predictable revenue.
- **Never discount without getting something.** "I can do 15% off if you sign a 2-year deal" or "if you commit by Friday."

### Handling objections

Objections aren't rejection — they're requests for more information.

| Objection | What they're really saying | Response approach |
|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| "It's too expensive" | I don't see enough value | Reframe in terms of ROI |
| "We need to think about it" | I'm not convinced yet | Identify the specific concern |
| "We're happy with our current solution" | The switching cost seems high | Quantify the cost of the status quo |
| "I need to talk to my boss" | I'm not the decision-maker | Ask to include them in the next call |
| "Can you add X feature?" | I'm interested but need customization | Understand if it's a must-have or nice-to-have |

### The champion model

In enterprise sales, you rarely sell to one person. You need a **champion** — someone inside the customer's organization who:

- Believes in your product
- Has influence
- Will sell internally on your behalf

Your job is to find the champion, arm them with the right materials (ROI analysis, slides, case studies), and help them win the internal argument.

## 5. Sales Metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Good benchmark |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Pipeline value | Total potential revenue in active deals | 3-4x your target (most won't close) |
| Win rate | % of opportunities that close | 20-30% (enterprise), 5-15% (SMB) |
| Average deal size | Revenue per closed deal | Should grow over time |
| Sales cycle length | Days from first contact to close | Shorter is better |
| Quota attainment | % of reps hitting their target | 60-70% means quotas are calibrated right |
| Revenue per rep | Revenue ÷ number of reps | Indicates sales efficiency |
| Churn rate | % of customers lost per period | < 5% annual for enterprise, < 10% for SMB |
| Net Revenue Retention | Revenue from existing customers (including expansion) ÷ prior period | > 100% means existing customers grow |

### The leaky bucket

```
New customers added:    +100
Customers churned:      - 30
Net new customers:      + 70
```

If your bucket leaks faster than you fill it, growth stalls. Retention is ultimately more important than acquisition — it's cheaper to keep a customer than to find a new one.

## 6. Sales Compensation

How you pay salespeople determines how they behave.

### The standard model

```
Total Compensation = Base Salary + Variable (Commission)
```

Typical split: 50/50 (base/variable) for account executives. More heavily weighted toward variable for closers. More toward base for customer success / account management.

### On-Target Earnings (OTE)

The total compensation if the rep hits 100% of quota.

Example: $80K base + $80K variable = $160K OTE with a 50/50 split.

### Commission structures

| Structure | How it works | When to use |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| % of revenue | Rep earns X% of each deal | Simple, aligned with revenue |
| % of ACV | Same but on annual contract value | SaaS, prevents gaming monthly deals |
| Tiered | Higher % after hitting quota | Rewards top performers, drives stretch |
| Accelerators | 2-3x commission rate above quota | Strongly incentivizes overperformance |
| Clawbacks | Commission reversed if customer churns | Prevents reps from closing bad deals |

### Incentive alignment

Compensation design is applied game theory (see [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md)):
- If you pay on closed revenue only, reps will promise anything to close — and churn will spike.
- If you pay on retention, reps will cherry-pick easy customers and avoid risk.
- The best comp plans balance new revenue, retention, and expansion.

## 7. Sales Operations and Tools

### The CRM (Customer Relationship Management)

The system of record for all sales activity. Tracks leads, opportunities, contacts, deals, and history.

Salesforce dominates enterprise. HubSpot serves SMB. Pipedrive, Close, and others serve startups.

The CRM is only useful if reps actually use it. The number one CRM problem isn't the software — it's data hygiene.

### The sales tech stack

| Tool category | What it does | Examples |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| CRM | Tracks deals and relationships | Salesforce, HubSpot |
| Sales engagement | Automates outreach sequences | Outreach, Salesloft |
| Call recording | Records and analyzes sales calls | Gong, Chorus |
| Prospecting | Finds contact info and company data | Apollo, ZoomInfo |
| CPQ | Configure, Price, Quote — generates proposals | Salesforce CPQ, DealHub |
| E-signature | Digital contract signing | DocuSign, PandaDoc |

## 8. Enterprise Sales — A Different Game

Selling to large companies is fundamentally different from selling to SMBs or consumers.

### What makes enterprise different

- **Multiple decision-makers** — You sell to a committee, not a person
- **Long cycles** — 3-12 months is normal
- **Security and compliance reviews** — SOC 2, GDPR, penetration tests
- **Procurement** — a department whose job is to reduce the price you charge
- **Legal review** — your contract will be redlined extensively
- **Integration requirements** — must work with their existing stack
- **Custom pricing** — list prices are a starting point for negotiation

### The enterprise sales cycle

```
Prospecting → Discovery → Technical Evaluation → Business Case →
Security Review → Legal/Procurement → Executive Approval → Close
```

Each stage has its own stakeholders, objections, and timelines. A deal can die at any stage.

### Multi-threading

Don't rely on a single contact. Build relationships with multiple people:
- **End user** — validates the product works
- **Champion** — sells internally on your behalf
- **Economic buyer** — controls the budget
- **Technical evaluator** — validates security and integration
- **Executive sponsor** — gives final approval

If your champion leaves the company, a single-threaded deal dies instantly.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Match your sales model to your price point.** Self-serve for low ACV. Field sales for high ACV. Don't use a $200K sales rep to close $20/month deals.
2. **Discovery is the most important step.** Understand the problem before pitching the solution.
3. **Price on value, not cost.** Anchor high, discount strategically.
4. **Metrics drive behavior.** What you measure and compensate for is what you'll get.
5. **Enterprise is a different game.** Multiple stakeholders, long cycles, complex processes. Multi-thread or die.
6. **Retention > acquisition.** A leaky bucket can't be filled. Fix churn before scaling sales.
