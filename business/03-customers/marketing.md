# Marketing — How Demand Finds Supply

> Marketing is not advertising. Advertising is one small tactic within marketing.
> Marketing is the entire discipline of understanding your customer, positioning
> your product in their mind, and creating the conditions under which they choose
> you over every alternative — including doing nothing.

**Prerequisites:** [Product](product.md) — you need something worth marketing. [Competitive Strategy](../02-strategy/competitive-strategy.md) — positioning comes from strategy. [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md) — understand demand.
**What it enables:** [Sales](sales.md) — marketing fills the top of the funnel. [Growth](../06-growth/growth-and-scaling.md) — marketing is the engine.

---

## Why This Matters

You can build the best product in the world and still fail if nobody knows about it. Conversely, mediocre products with brilliant marketing can dominate markets for years.

Marketing is not about tricking people into buying things they don't want. It's about connecting the right product with the right customer at the right time with the right message. That's hard. And doing it systematically, repeatedly, at scale, is one of the most valuable skills in business.

## 1. Positioning — The Foundation of All Marketing

Before you write a single ad or send a single email, you need to answer: **What are you, and for whom?**

### April Dunford's positioning framework

Five questions that define your position:

1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — What do you have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value** — What does that uniqueness mean for the customer?
4. **Target customers** — Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market category** — What context makes your value obvious?

### Positioning statement template

```
For [target customer]
who [has this problem],
[product name] is a [market category]
that [key benefit].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
we [key differentiator].
```

Example:
"For remote engineering teams who struggle with async communication, Loom is a video messaging tool that replaces long email threads with quick videos. Unlike Zoom, you don't need everyone online at the same time."

### The positioning trap

Most founders describe their product by what it *does* (features). Customers care about what it *does for them* (benefits).

| Feature | Benefit |
|---------|---------|
| 256-bit encryption | Your data is safe |
| 99.99% uptime SLA | Your app never goes down |
| AI-powered recommendations | You find what you want faster |
| Real-time collaboration | Your team works together, not in sequence |

Always translate features into benefits.

## 2. The Marketing Funnel

The customer journey from stranger to buyer to advocate:

```
                 AWARENESS
              ╱            ╲
           INTEREST          "I've heard of them"
          ╱        ╲
      CONSIDERATION  "I'm evaluating options"
       ╱            ╲
    INTENT            "I think I want this"
     ╱      ╲
  PURCHASE    "I'm buying"
   ╱    ╲
LOYALTY   "I'll buy again"
  ╱  ╲
ADVOCACY  "I'm telling everyone"
```

Different marketing activities serve different stages:

| Stage | Tactics |
|-------|---------|
| Awareness | Content marketing, SEO, social media, PR, ads |
| Interest | Blog posts, webinars, free tools, lead magnets |
| Consideration | Case studies, comparisons, demos, free trials |
| Intent | Pricing pages, sales calls, proposals |
| Purchase | Checkout optimization, onboarding |
| Loyalty | Customer success, email nurturing, community |
| Advocacy | Referral programs, reviews, community |

The most common mistake: spending all your effort on awareness and none on the bottom of the funnel. Getting traffic is worthless if it doesn't convert.

## 3. Customer Acquisition Channels

### Content marketing & SEO

Create valuable content that attracts your target audience through search.

**How it works:** Write content about problems your customers have → Google indexes it → customers find you when searching for solutions → they discover your product.

- **Strengths:** Compounding returns (content keeps working forever), builds authority, low marginal cost
- **Weaknesses:** Takes months to gain traction, requires consistency, competitive keywords are hard to rank for
- **Key metrics:** Organic traffic, keyword rankings, traffic-to-signup conversion

### Paid advertising

Pay to put your message in front of potential customers.

Channels:
- **Search ads (Google Ads)** — high intent ("best CRM software" = someone actively looking)
- **Social ads (Meta, LinkedIn, Twitter)** — demand generation (reaching people who aren't looking yet)
- **Display ads** — brand awareness, retargeting
- **Programmatic** — automated ad buying across networks

- **Strengths:** Immediate results, precise targeting, scalable with budget
- **Weaknesses:** Expensive, stops working when you stop paying, increasingly competitive
- **Key metrics:** CPC (cost per click), CPA (cost per acquisition), ROAS (return on ad spend)

### Social media

Build an audience and engage directly.

- **Organic social** — building followers and community without paying
- **Paid social** — boosting content or running targeted ads
- **Community** — Discord, Slack, forums where your users gather

Best for: brand awareness, thought leadership, B2C engagement.

### Email marketing

Still one of the highest-ROI channels. You own the list (unlike social media followers).

- **Newsletter** — regular value delivery to subscribers
- **Drip campaigns** — automated sequences triggered by actions
- **Transactional email** — receipts, onboarding, notifications that also serve marketing goals

Key metrics: open rate (20-30% is good), click rate (2-5% is good), unsubscribe rate (< 0.5%).

### Word of mouth / referrals

The most trusted and cheapest channel. But you can't manufacture it — you earn it with a great product and great experience.

You can *amplify* it with:
- Referral programs (Dropbox: invite a friend, both get storage)
- Reviews and testimonials
- Community building
- Products designed to be shared (Figma, Notion, Canva)

### Partnerships and distribution

Borrow someone else's audience.

- **Co-marketing** — joint webinars, content, campaigns
- **Channel partners** — resellers, integrators, consultants
- **Platform distribution** — app stores, marketplaces, integrations
- **API partnerships** — your product embedded in someone else's

### PR and media

Getting press coverage. Effective for launches and major milestones. Not a sustained channel for most companies.

## 4. Brand — More Than a Logo

Brand is the set of associations, feelings, and expectations people have about your company. It's not what you say you are — it's what they think you are.

### Brand components

| Component | What it is |
|-----------|-----------|
| Name | What you're called |
| Visual identity | Logo, colors, typography, design system |
| Voice and tone | How you sound in writing and speech |
| Values | What you stand for |
| Promise | What customers can consistently expect |
| Story | Why you exist and where you're going |

### Why brand matters

In commodity markets, brand is the differentiator. When two products are functionally identical, people buy the one they trust, recognize, or identify with.

Brand also reduces CAC. A strong brand means people seek you out (pull) instead of you chasing them (push). Apple doesn't need to explain what an iPhone is.

### Brand building takes time

Brand is a long-term investment. It compounds like interest. It can't be bought overnight with advertising. And it can be destroyed in a day.

## 5. Content Strategy

Content marketing is the practice of creating and distributing valuable content to attract and retain a defined audience.

### The content hierarchy

```
                    ╱╲
                   ╱  ╲
                  ╱ 10x ╲     Definitive guides, original research
                 ╱ Content╲    (high effort, massive reach)
                ╱──────────╲
               ╱            ╲
              ╱  Cornerstone  ╲  Comprehensive posts on core topics
             ╱    Content      ╲ (medium effort, strong SEO)
            ╱──────────────────╲
           ╱                    ╲
          ╱    Regular Content    ╲  Blog posts, updates, tutorials
         ╱                        ╲ (low-medium effort, consistent traffic)
        ╱──────────────────────────╲
       ╱                            ╲
      ╱       Social & Engagement     ╲ Tweets, comments, short posts
     ╱                                  ╲(low effort, engagement & reach)
    ╱────────────────────────────────────╲
```

### Content types by funnel stage

| Funnel stage | Content type | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Awareness | Blog posts, infographics, videos | "The Complete Guide to Remote Work" |
| Consideration | Case studies, comparisons, webinars | "How Company X Increased Retention by 40%" |
| Decision | Product demos, free trials, pricing guides | "See How [Product] Works in 3 Minutes" |
| Retention | Tutorials, docs, tips emails | "5 Features You Didn't Know About" |

### The distribution problem

Creating content is half the job. Distribution is the other half. Most content fails not because it's bad but because nobody sees it.

Distribution channels for content:
- SEO (organic search)
- Email newsletter
- Social media (organic + paid)
- Communities (Reddit, Hacker News, industry forums)
- Syndication (Medium, LinkedIn articles)
- Partnerships (guest posts, cross-promotion)

## 6. Key Marketing Metrics

| Metric | What it measures | Why it matters |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| CAC | Cost to acquire one customer | Are you spending efficiently? |
| LTV | Lifetime value of a customer | Does acquisition pay off? |
| LTV/CAC | Return on acquisition spend | Is the model working? (need ≥ 3x) |
| Conversion rate | % who take the desired action | Is your funnel working? |
| ROAS | Revenue per dollar of ad spend | Are ads profitable? |
| Organic traffic | Visitors from search | Is content/SEO working? |
| MQL → SQL conversion | Marketing lead to sales lead | Are you generating quality leads? |
| NPS | How likely customers recommend you | Do customers like you enough to refer? |

### Attribution — the unsolvable problem

When a customer buys, which marketing touchpoint gets credit?

- They saw a blog post (content)
- Then a retargeting ad (paid)
- Then a webinar (events)
- Then a sales call (sales)

Who gets credit? There's no perfect answer. Common models:
- **First-touch** — credit the first interaction
- **Last-touch** — credit the last interaction before purchase
- **Multi-touch** — distribute credit across all touchpoints
- **Self-reported** — ask "how did you hear about us?"

No model is right. Use a combination and accept imperfection.

## 7. B2B vs. B2C Marketing

| Dimension | B2B | B2C |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Buyer | Committee / multiple stakeholders | Individual |
| Decision cycle | Weeks to months | Minutes to days |
| Decision driver | ROI, efficiency, risk reduction | Emotion, convenience, price |
| Content | Whitepapers, case studies, webinars | Social media, video, influencers |
| Channels | LinkedIn, email, events, sales | Instagram, TikTok, Google, retail |
| Pricing | Custom, negotiated | Fixed, transparent |
| Relationships | Long-term, high-touch | Transactional (usually) |

B2B marketing generates *leads* that sales closes. B2C marketing often drives the purchase directly.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Positioning is the foundation.** If you can't explain what you are and why someone should care, no tactic saves you.
2. **Features tell, benefits sell.** Always translate what it does into what it does *for the customer*.
3. **Content compounds.** SEO and content marketing are slow but create durable, owned distribution.
4. **CAC must be justified by LTV.** Marketing that acquires unprofitable customers is just expensive brand awareness.
5. **Distribution > content.** Great content with no distribution is a tree falling in an empty forest.
6. **Brand is a long-term moat.** It can't be built overnight, but once built, it reduces CAC and increases trust.
