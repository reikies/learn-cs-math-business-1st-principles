# Marketing & Distribution — Getting People to Know You Exist

> The hardest part of making money isn't building — it's getting people to know
> your thing exists. You can build the best product in the world and die in
> obscurity. Distribution is not a side quest. It's the main quest. The builders
> who understand this win. The ones who don't, build beautiful things that
> nobody uses.

**Prerequisites:** [Product](product.md) — you need something worth distributing. [Product Thinking](product-thinking.md) — you need to have built the right thing. [Competitive Strategy](../02-strategy/competitive-strategy.md) — positioning comes from strategy.
**What it enables:** Revenue. [Sales](sales.md), [Growth & Scaling](../06-growth/growth-and-scaling.md), [The Startup Journey](../07-entrepreneurship/the-startup-journey.md).

---

## Why This Matters

Every founder thinks their challenge is building the product. The real challenge is distribution.

The world is full of products that are technically superior to their market leaders. Google wasn't the first search engine. Facebook wasn't the first social network. Slack wasn't the first chat tool. They won on distribution — getting the right product in front of the right people at the right time through the right channel.

This is doubly true for AI products. The technology is accessible to everyone with an API key. The model is not your moat. Your ability to reach customers, educate them on why they need your product, and get them to switch from their current workflow — that's the moat.

## 1. The Distribution Mindset

### Build vs. distribute

```
What most builders think:
    Build amazing product → People find it → Growth

What actually happens:
    Build good-enough product → Fight like hell for distribution →
    Learn from users → Improve product → Fight for more distribution →
    Eventually: product + distribution = compounding growth
```

Distribution is not something you bolt on after building. It's something you design into the product and company from day one.

### First-mover advantage is a distribution advantage

Being first doesn't matter. Being first to reach customers at scale matters.

Google wasn't the first search engine (AltaVista, Lycos, Yahoo were). But Google got distribution through:
- Firefox default search deal
- Android (built their own distribution platform)
- Chrome (built another one)
- Google.com as homepage on every ISP's browser

Product quality got them in the door. Distribution made them dominant.

### The distribution hierarchy

```
Strongest distribution (you own the channel):
│
│  Built into the product (virality, network effects)
│  Your own audience (email list, community, social following)
│  Your own platform (app store, marketplace, API ecosystem)
│  Organic search (SEO — you earn it, but Google controls it)
│  Partnerships (shared but somewhat stable)
│  Paid channels (you rent it, stops when you stop paying)
│  Press / PR (temporary spike, not sustained)
│
Weakest distribution (you rent the channel)
```

The best companies own their distribution. The most fragile companies depend entirely on rented channels.

## 2. Content Marketing — Educating the Market

### Why content matters for technology products

When you're selling a new category — especially AI tools — your first job isn't selling. It's **educating**. People can't buy what they don't understand. They can't want what they don't know exists.

Content marketing solves the education problem while simultaneously building:
- **Trust** — you demonstrate expertise before asking for money
- **SEO** — content ranks in search, creating permanent distribution
- **Brand** — consistent, valuable content builds recognition over time
- **Community** — good content attracts people who share your worldview

### The content engine

```
Identify audience pain points
    │
    ▼
Create content that addresses those pains
(blog posts, videos, tutorials, guides)
    │
    ▼
Distribute through channels
(search, social, email, communities)
    │
    ▼
Audience discovers content → gets value → trusts you
    │
    ▼
Some fraction converts (email signup, free trial, purchase)
    │
    ▼
Learn what resonates → create more of it
    │
    └──── feeds back to the top ────┘
```

### Content types ranked by impact

| Content type | Effort | Longevity | Distribution | Best for |
|-------------|--------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Definitive guides** | Very high | Years | SEO, sharing | Establishing authority on a topic |
| **Original research / data** | High | Years | PR, social, backlinks | Standing out — nobody else has this data |
| **Tutorials & how-tos** | Medium | Months-years | SEO, communities | Reaching people actively solving a problem |
| **Thought leadership** | Medium | Months | Social, email | Building personal brand and trust |
| **Case studies** | Medium | Months-years | Sales enablement, SEO | Proving your product works for real companies |
| **News commentary** | Low | Days-weeks | Social, email | Staying relevant and visible |
| **Social posts** | Low | Hours-days | Platform algorithm | Engagement, reach, top of mind |

### The content moat for AI companies

AI products especially need content because:

1. **The market needs education.** Most potential customers don't understand what AI can do for them specifically. Content bridges that gap.

2. **Trust is low.** AI hype created skepticism. "AI-powered" is noise. Showing, not telling, through content (demos, case studies, comparisons) builds trust.

3. **Use cases aren't obvious.** Unlike a CRM or email tool, the applications of AI vary wildly by industry and workflow. Content maps specific use cases to specific audiences.

4. **The technology changes fast.** Content that explains what's newly possible — and what's still hype — positions you as a trusted guide.

## 3. SEO & Organic Growth

### Why SEO is the best distribution channel (for most products)

SEO (Search Engine Optimization) compounds. A blog post that ranks #1 for a search term sends traffic every day, for months or years, for free. It's the closest thing to a perpetual motion machine in marketing.

```
Month 1:  Publish 10 articles. Traffic: ~100 visits.
Month 6:  Articles start ranking. Traffic: ~2,000 visits.
Month 12: Top articles dominate. Traffic: ~10,000 visits.
Month 24: Compounding. Traffic: ~50,000 visits.

Compare to paid ads:
Month 1:  Spend $10K. Traffic: 5,000 visits.
Month 2:  Spend $10K. Traffic: 5,000 visits.
Month 12: Spend $10K. Traffic: 5,000 visits.
Stop paying: Traffic: 0.
```

### How SEO actually works

```
1. Keyword research
   "What are people searching for that relates to my product?"
   │
2. Content creation
   "Create the best answer on the internet for that search"
   │
3. Technical SEO
   "Make sure Google can find, read, and index my content"
   │
4. Authority building
   "Earn backlinks from other sites that vouch for your content"
   │
5. Measurement & iteration
   "Track rankings, traffic, and conversions. Double down on what works."
```

### Keyword strategy for technology products

| Keyword type | Example | Intent | Conversion | Competition |
|-------------|---------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Problem-aware** | "how to automate customer support" | Searching for solutions | Medium | Medium |
| **Solution-aware** | "best AI customer support tools" | Comparing options | High | High |
| **Brand** | "Intercom vs Zendesk" | Ready to decide | Very high | Medium |
| **Educational** | "what is natural language processing" | Learning | Low | Low |
| **Long-tail** | "how to use AI for SaaS onboarding emails" | Specific need | High | Low |

The strategy: dominate long-tail and problem-aware keywords first (less competition, higher relevance), then move up to solution-aware and brand comparison keywords as your authority grows.

### The SEO flywheel

```
Create valuable content → Ranks in Google → Traffic arrives →
Some visitors link to your content → More backlinks → Higher authority →
Higher rankings → More traffic → More links → ...
```

This flywheel takes 6-12 months to start spinning. Most companies give up at month 3. The ones who persist build an enormous, durable distribution advantage.

## 4. Community Building — Your Most Defensible Channel

### Why community is distribution

A community is a group of people who share an identity, a set of problems, or a worldview — and who gather around your brand.

Community is distribution because:
- **Members recruit other members** (organic growth)
- **Members create content** (UGC scales your content operation)
- **Members defend your brand** (in competitive discussions)
- **Members give you product feedback** (faster and more honest than surveys)
- **Members don't leave easily** (switching costs are social, not just functional)

### Developer communities vs. user communities

| Dimension | Developer community | User community |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Where they gather** | GitHub, Discord, Stack Overflow, Hacker News | Facebook Groups, Reddit, Slack, Forums |
| **What they value** | Documentation, open source, technical depth, hackability | Ease of use, templates, tutorials, peer support |
| **How to earn trust** | Ship great docs, respond to issues, be technically excellent | Be responsive, share genuine expertise, highlight member wins |
| **What kills trust** | Marketing speak, vendor lock-in, ignoring community feedback | Spam, selling too hard, ignoring complaints |
| **Community content** | Code examples, plugins, integrations, technical blog posts | Success stories, workflows, templates, Q&A |

### Building a community from scratch

```
Phase 1: Gather (0-100 members)
├── Find where your audience already hangs out
├── Participate genuinely (don't sell)
├── Invite early users to a private space (Discord, Slack)
└── Be the most active member — answer every question

Phase 2: Cultivate (100-1,000 members)
├── Create recurring rituals (weekly threads, office hours, AMAs)
├── Spotlight community members (not yourself)
├── Start producing community-specific content
└── Identify and empower super-users as moderators

Phase 3: Scale (1,000-10,000+ members)
├── Community generates its own content and conversations
├── You shift from creator to curator and facilitator
├── Launch community programs (ambassadors, certifications, events)
└── The community becomes a distribution channel for your product

Phase 4: Ecosystem (10,000+)
├── Third parties build on your platform/product
├── Community members teach each other
├── The community is self-sustaining — it doesn't collapse if you stop posting
└── Your community IS your moat
```

### Examples

| Company | Community type | How it drives distribution |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Figma** | Designer community | Shared files are free marketing. Community templates onboard new users. |
| **Notion** | User community | Template creators build a content ecosystem. Reddit community drives organic discovery. |
| **Vercel / Next.js** | Developer community | Open source creates evangelists. Dev community = distribution to technical decision-makers. |
| **Salesforce** | Trailblazer community | Certified admins become internal advocates at every company they join. |
| **HubSpot** | Marketing community | HubSpot Academy trains marketers on HubSpot's methodology (and tools). |

## 5. Product-Led Growth vs. Sales-Led Growth

### The fundamental choice

How do people discover and adopt your product?

```
Product-Led Growth (PLG):
User finds product → tries it (free/freemium) → gets value →
invites others → team adopts → company pays

Sales-Led Growth (SLG):
Marketing generates lead → sales rep contacts them → demo →
evaluation → negotiation → contract → implementation → adoption
```

### PLG: when the product sells itself

**How it works:**
The product is the primary acquisition, conversion, and expansion vehicle. Users can sign up, get value, and pay without ever talking to a human.

**Characteristics:**
- Free trial or freemium tier
- Self-serve onboarding (value in minutes, not weeks)
- Usage naturally spreads (collaboration, sharing, virality)
- Revenue expands as usage grows (usage-based or seat-based pricing)
- Sales team focuses on expansion and enterprise, not initial acquisition

**Examples:**

| Company | PLG mechanism | Why it works |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Slack** | Team communication is inherently collaborative | Every message sent to an external person is an invitation |
| **Figma** | Design files are shared for feedback | Reviewers become users who become creators |
| **Calendly** | Every meeting invite shows the product | The recipient sees Calendly in action without signing up |
| **Notion** | Templates are shared publicly | Discovering a useful template → signing up → creating your own |
| **Zoom** | Joining a meeting requires no account | "This is so easy" → host your own meetings |
| **Canva** | Beautiful designs shared on social media | "Made with Canva" → others want to make their own |

**The PLG flywheel:**

```
User signs up (free) → Gets value quickly → Uses product regularly →
  → Invites collaborators → They sign up → They invite others →
    → Usage grows → Hits free tier limits → Upgrades to paid →
      → Expanded usage → More invites → More users → ...
```

### SLG: when you need to guide the sale

**How it works:**
Sales reps drive the buying process. Marketing generates awareness and leads, but a human guides the prospect from interest to purchase.

**Characteristics:**
- High average contract value ($10K+/year)
- Complex product requiring explanation or customization
- Multiple stakeholders in the buying decision
- Long evaluation and procurement cycles
- Implementation requires support

**When SLG is the right choice:**

| Condition | Why SLG wins |
|-----------|-------------|
| Product is complex | Users can't self-serve — they need a guided experience |
| ACV is high | The economics support a sales team ($50K+ deals) |
| Buyers ≠ users | The person paying isn't the person using (enterprise IT) |
| Switching costs are high | Customers need handholding through migration |
| Trust is critical | Healthcare, finance, government — they need a human |
| Market needs education | New category — customers don't know they need it yet |

### The hybrid: PLG + Sales

Most successful companies evolve to a hybrid. The product creates bottom-up adoption. Sales captures top-down revenue.

```
Individual user finds product → adopts free tier
    │
    ▼
Team adopts → usage grows across department
    │
    ▼
Sales identifies expansion signal (usage spike, team invites)
    │
    ▼
Sales engages → enterprise deal → company-wide rollout
    │
    ▼
Customer success ensures adoption → expansion → renewal
```

This is how Slack, Datadog, Figma, and Notion work. PLG creates the initial wedge. Sales expands it into enterprise revenue.

### How to choose

```
                    Product complexity
                    Low ────────────── High
                    │                    │
              Low   │   Self-serve       │   Inside sales
    Price     ACV   │   (pure PLG)       │   (PLG + sales)
    point           │                    │
              High  │   PLG with         │   Field sales
              ACV   │   sales assist     │   (pure SLG)
                    │                    │
```

## 6. Channel Strategy — Where to Find Your Customers

### The bullseye framework

From Gabriel Weinberg (Traction): test many channels, find the one that works, then double down.

```
        Outer ring: What's possible (all channels)
       ╱                                            ╲
      ╱   Middle ring: What's probable (6 channels)  ╲
     ╱  ╱                                          ╲  ╲
    ╱  ╱   Inner ring: What works (1-2 channels)    ╲  ╲
   ╱  ╱  ╱                                      ╲  ╲  ╲
  │  │  │            🎯 BULLSEYE                  │  │  │
   ╲  ╲  ╲                                      ╱  ╱  ╱
    ╲  ╲   ╲                                  ╱  ╱  ╱
     ╲  ╲    ────────────────────────────────  ╱  ╱
      ╲  ────────────────────────────────────  ╱
       ──────────────────────────────────────
```

**Step 1:** Brainstorm all possible channels (outer ring)
**Step 2:** Pick 6 with the best potential and run cheap tests (middle ring)
**Step 3:** Identify the 1-2 that actually work and pour resources into them (bullseye)

### Channel matrix for tech products

| Channel | Best for | Time to results | Cost | Sustainability |
|---------|---------|----------------|------|---------------|
| **Content / SEO** | Products with searchable pain points | 6-12 months | Low-medium | Very high (compounds) |
| **Social media (organic)** | Products with visual/shareable output | 3-6 months | Low (but time-intensive) | Medium (algorithm-dependent) |
| **Paid search (Google)** | Products people actively search for | Immediate | High | Low (stops when you stop paying) |
| **Paid social (Meta, LinkedIn)** | Products that create demand | Immediate | High | Low |
| **Community (Discord, Reddit)** | Developer tools, niche products | 6-12 months | Low | Very high |
| **Partnerships** | Products that complement other products | 3-6 months | Low-medium | High |
| **Product Hunt / HN launches** | Developer and tech early adopter products | Immediate (spike) | Low | Very low (one-time) |
| **Email outreach** | B2B with clear ICP | Weeks | Low | Medium |
| **Events & conferences** | Enterprise, B2B | Months | High | Medium |
| **Referral programs** | Products with inherent virality | Weeks-months | Medium | High if K > 0.5 |
| **Open source** | Developer tools, infrastructure | 6-12 months | High (engineering time) | Very high |

### The law of channel concentration

At any given time, one channel drives 80%+ of your growth. Find it and own it before diversifying.

Companies that try to be everywhere are usually strong nowhere. The right move at each stage:

```
Pre-PMF:     Test many channels cheaply. Find the one that works.
Post-PMF:    Double down on the winning channel. Become the best at it.
Scaling:     Add one channel at a time. Don't add the next until the
             current one is systemized and delegated.
At scale:    Multiple channels, but still with clear priorities.
```

## 7. Distribution for AI Products — Specific Tactics

### The AI distribution challenge

AI products face unique distribution problems:

1. **"AI" has become noise.** Every product claims to be AI-powered. The term has lost meaning. You can't differentiate on "AI" — you differentiate on the *outcome*.

2. **Use cases aren't obvious.** For a CRM, people know what it does. For "AI for X," you have to explain what's possible.

3. **The technology changes weekly.** Your marketing messages about capabilities become outdated fast.

4. **Demo → trial conversion is hard.** AI demos look magical. Reality is messier. Expectation management is a distribution problem.

### What works for AI products

**Show, don't tell.**
Record screen captures of your product in action. Side-by-side comparisons (manual process vs. your AI). Before/after demonstrations. Let the product speak.

**Educate through content.**
Write about the *problem*, not the technology. "How to cut customer support response time by 80%" not "Our transformer-based NLP model achieves state-of-the-art performance."

**Build in public.**
Share your development journey, interesting findings, honest results (including limitations). The AI community rewards transparency and punishes hype.

**Leverage AI-native communities.**
Hacker News, Reddit (r/artificial, r/machinelearning, subreddits for your domain), Twitter/X AI community, Discord servers. These communities discover and amplify products they genuinely find useful.

**Free tier with a clear upgrade path.**
Let people experience the AI before asking them to pay. The "aha moment" for AI products is experiential — no amount of marketing copy can replace the feeling of the AI actually solving your problem.

**Integration over destination.**
Don't ask users to come to your app. Go to where they already are. Browser extensions, API integrations, Slack bots, editor plugins. Distribution through integration is the most natural path for AI tools.

## 8. Measuring Distribution

### The metrics that matter

| Metric | What it tells you | Why it matters |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **Traffic sources** | Where your visitors come from | Are you building owned or rented distribution? |
| **Organic traffic growth** | Month-over-month search traffic | Is your content/SEO compounding? |
| **Signup rate** | Visitors → signups | Is your landing page converting? |
| **Activation rate** | Signups → "aha moment" | Are people experiencing the core value? |
| **Channel CAC** | Cost to acquire via each channel | Which channels are efficient? |
| **Viral coefficient (K)** | Users generated per existing user | Is the product spreading? |
| **Payback period** | Months to recoup CAC | Can you afford to keep spending? |
| **Time to value** | How fast new users get value | Speed = higher activation = better distribution |

### The distribution audit

Ask yourself quarterly:

1. **If our best channel disappeared tomorrow, would we survive?** If your entire distribution depends on Google SEO, one algorithm change could wipe you out. Diversification isn't urgent, but it's important.

2. **Are we building owned or rented distribution?** Email lists, communities, and product virality are owned. Paid ads and social media algorithms are rented.

3. **What's our distribution advantage over competitors?** If anyone can buy the same ads and write the same blog posts, your distribution isn't a moat. What do you have that they don't?

4. **Is our content generating compounding returns?** If content from 6 months ago still drives traffic and signups, the engine is working. If only this month's content drives results, you're on a treadmill.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Distribution is the main quest, not a side quest.** The best product with the worst distribution loses to an okay product with the best distribution.
2. **Content marketing is the highest-ROI channel for most tech products.** It compounds, builds trust, and creates permanent distribution.
3. **SEO takes time but is worth it.** 6-12 months to start working. Then it compounds for years. Most companies quit too early.
4. **Community is the most defensible distribution channel.** Members recruit, create content, and advocate. It's slow to build but nearly impossible for competitors to replicate.
5. **PLG and SLG aren't mutually exclusive.** The best companies use PLG for bottom-up adoption and sales for top-down expansion.
6. **Find one channel that works before diversifying.** Concentration beats diversification at every stage before scale.
7. **For AI products: show, don't tell.** Educate the market through content. Let the product demo itself. Meet users where they already work.
