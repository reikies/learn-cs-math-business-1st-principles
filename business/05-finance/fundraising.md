# Fundraising & Venture Capital — Trading Equity for Acceleration

> Raising money is not a milestone — it's a tool. A fundraise means you've convinced
> someone that the future value of your company justifies giving you their money today.
> It's not validation. It's not success. It's fuel. And fuel without a destination
> is just a fire.

**Prerequisites:** [Legal Foundations](../01-foundations/legal-foundations.md) — entities, securities law, cap tables. [Accounting](../01-foundations/accounting.md) — financial literacy. [Corporate Finance](corporate-finance.md) — valuation, equity.
**What it enables:** Growth, hiring, product development — everything that costs money. [Growth & Scaling](../06-growth/growth-and-scaling.md).

---

## Why This Matters

Some of the greatest companies in history were venture-funded: Google, Amazon, Apple, Facebook, Airbnb. Others were bootstrapped: Mailchimp, Basecamp, Patagonia, Spanx.

Neither path is inherently better. But if you choose to raise venture capital, you need to understand what you're signing up for — the mechanics, the incentives, the trade-offs, and the game theory. Founders who don't understand fundraising get diluted, outvoted, or fired from their own companies.

## 1. Bootstrapping vs. Raising — The First Decision

### Bootstrapping

Fund the company from your own savings and revenue.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Full ownership and control | Slower growth |
| No outside pressure on timeline | Limited by revenue and savings |
| Forced profitability discipline | Can't take big market bets |
| No dilution | Harder to attract talent (less equity upside) |
| No board to answer to | Opportunity cost if market window closes |

### Venture capital

Sell equity to investors in exchange for capital.

| Pros | Cons |
|------|------|
| Large capital for fast growth | Dilution (you own less) |
| Smart money (network, expertise, brand) | Loss of control (board seats, veto rights) |
| Can take big market bets | Pressure to grow at all costs |
| Attracts talent (equity upside) | VC timeline: must exit in 7-10 years |
| Credibility signal | Not all VCs add value beyond money |

### When to raise

Raise venture capital when:
- The market opportunity is large and time-sensitive
- Capital is the primary constraint (not idea, not execution)
- Winner-take-all dynamics reward speed
- You need to invest before you can earn (e.g., building a marketplace)

Bootstrap when:
- You can get to revenue quickly
- The market doesn't require blitz-scaling
- You value control and independence
- The unit economics work without subsidizing growth

## 2. How Venture Capital Works

### The VC fund structure

VCs don't invest their own money. They raise a **fund** from **Limited Partners (LPs)** — pension funds, endowments, family offices, sovereign wealth funds.

```
LPs (pension funds, endowments)
    │
    │  commit capital
    ▼
VC Fund (managed by General Partners)
    │
    │  invest in startups
    ▼
Portfolio Companies (startups)
    │
    │  grow, exit (IPO or acquisition)
    ▼
Returns flow back: Companies → Fund → LPs (and GPs take 20%)
```

### The VC business model

```
Management fee:  2% of fund per year (pays salaries, operations)
Carry:           20% of profits (the incentive)
```

A $200M fund generates $4M/year in management fees. If the fund returns $600M ($400M profit), the GPs earn $80M in carry.

### The power law

This is the most important thing to understand about VC incentives:

**VC returns follow a power law.** A tiny number of investments generate almost all the returns. One company returning 100x makes up for dozens of failures.

What this means for you:
- VCs need you to have the *potential* to be a massive outcome
- A $100M exit is uninteresting to most VCs — it doesn't move the needle on a $500M fund
- VCs will push you to grow aggressively because they need you to be the fund-returner
- Small, profitable businesses are great — but they're not what VCs invest in

## 3. Funding Stages

### The typical path

```
Pre-Seed → Seed → Series A → Series B → Series C → ... → IPO or Acquisition
```

| Stage | Typical raise | Typical valuation | What you should have | Typical investors |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Pre-Seed | $250K-$1M | $2-5M | Idea, team, maybe a prototype | Angels, pre-seed funds |
| Seed | $1-4M | $5-15M | MVP, early users, some signal | Seed funds, angels, some VCs |
| Series A | $5-15M | $20-60M | PMF, repeatable growth, revenue | VCs |
| Series B | $15-50M | $60-200M | Proven unit economics, scaling | VCs, growth equity |
| Series C+ | $50M+ | $200M+ | Market leadership, path to profitability or IPO | Growth equity, crossover funds |

These numbers vary wildly by market, geography, and era. The 2021 numbers were 2-3x higher than the 2023-2024 numbers.

### What happens at each stage

**Pre-Seed/Seed:** Prove the problem exists and you can solve it. Build MVP. Find early users.

**Series A:** Prove you've found product-market fit and can grow. Show repeatable acquisition. Demonstrate unit economics.

**Series B:** Prove you can scale. Expand the team, enter new markets, build the growth machine.

**Series C and beyond:** Dominate the market. Build moats. Prepare for IPO or acquisition.

## 4. The Mechanics of a Round

### Key terms

| Term | What it means |
|------|-------------|
| Pre-money valuation | Company's value *before* the investment |
| Post-money valuation | Company's value *after* the investment (pre-money + investment amount) |
| Dilution | The % by which existing shareholders' ownership decreases |
| Pro rata rights | Investor's right to invest in future rounds to maintain their % |
| Board seat | Investor gets a seat on the board of directors |
| Liquidation preference | In a sale, investor gets their money back before common shareholders |
| Anti-dilution | Protects investor if the next round is at a lower valuation ("down round") |
| Vesting | Founder's equity is earned over time (usually 4 years) |

### The math of dilution

Example:

```
Before Seed:
  Founders own 10M shares (100%)

Seed round: Raise $2M at $8M pre-money ($10M post-money)
  New shares issued: 2.5M
  Founders: 10M / 12.5M = 80%
  Seed investor: 2.5M / 12.5M = 20%

Series A: Raise $10M at $40M pre-money ($50M post-money)
  New shares issued: 3.125M
  Founders: 10M / 15.625M = 64%
  Seed investor: 2.5M / 15.625M = 16%
  Series A investor: 3.125M / 15.625M = 20%

Option pool: Reserve 10% for employee stock options
  Founders: ~57%
  Seed: ~14%
  Series A: ~18%
  Option pool: ~10%
```

After two rounds and an option pool, founders own ~57%. After a Series B, they might own 40-45%. By IPO, founder ownership is often 10-20%.

This is normal. 20% of a $10B company is better than 100% of a $0 company.

### SAFEs and convertible notes

Early-stage fundraising often uses simplified instruments:

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity):** Investor gives you money now. It converts to equity at the next priced round. Key terms:
- **Valuation cap** — maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts
- **Discount** — percentage discount to the next round's price

Example: SAFE with $10M cap. Series A happens at $20M valuation. SAFE converts at $10M valuation — the investor gets 2x as many shares as the Series A investors.

**Convertible note:** Similar to a SAFE but it's debt — it has an interest rate and maturity date. If no equity round happens before maturity, the company technically owes the money back.

SAFEs are more founder-friendly (no maturity, no interest). YC popularized them, and they're now standard for pre-seed and seed rounds.

## 5. The Fundraising Process

### Timeline (typically 2-4 months)

```
Preparation → Targeting → Outreach → Meetings → Term Sheet →
  Due Diligence → Legal → Close → Wire
```

### The pitch

What investors need to hear:

1. **Problem** — What pain exists? How big is it?
2. **Solution** — What do you do about it?
3. **Market** — How big is the opportunity? (TAM, SAM, SOM)
4. **Traction** — What have you achieved so far?
5. **Business model** — How do you make money?
6. **Team** — Why are you the right people?
7. **Ask** — How much are you raising? What will you do with it?

### What investors evaluate

| Factor | What they're looking for |
|--------|------------------------|
| Team | Can these founders execute? Domain expertise? Grit? |
| Market | Is this market large enough to support a big outcome? |
| Product | Does it work? Do users love it? |
| Traction | Growth rate matters more than absolute numbers |
| Unit economics | Is the business model viable? |
| Defensibility | Moats, network effects, switching costs |
| Timing | Why now? What's changed that makes this possible today? |

### Negotiating the term sheet

Key things to negotiate:
- **Valuation** — higher is better for founders (less dilution)
- **Board composition** — keep founder control as long as possible
- **Liquidation preference** — 1x non-participating is standard. Anything higher is investor-friendly.
- **Anti-dilution** — broad-based weighted average is standard. Full ratchet is aggressive.
- **Pro rata rights** — standard for lead investors
- **Protective provisions** — investor veto rights on major decisions

The most important thing: **Don't optimize for valuation alone.** A higher valuation with bad terms (2x liquidation preference, full ratchet, board control) is worse than a lower valuation with clean terms.

## 6. Investor Types

| Type | Check size | Stage | Value-add |
|------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Friends & Family | $5K-$100K | Pre-seed | Emotional support. High risk for relationships. |
| Angel investors | $25K-$250K | Pre-seed, Seed | Advice, connections. Often former founders. |
| Accelerators (YC, Techstars) | $125K-$500K | Pre-seed | Program, network, brand, demo day. |
| Micro VCs | $500K-$2M | Seed | Specialized focus. Personal relationships. |
| VC firms | $2M-$100M+ | Seed through growth | Board seats, recruiting help, follow-on capital. |
| Corporate VC (CVC) | Varies | Any | Strategic value, partnerships. Watch for conflicts. |
| Growth equity | $50M+ | Series C+ | Scaling, IPO preparation. |
| Sovereign wealth / pension | $100M+ | Late stage | Patient capital, large checks. |

### Choosing investors

Not all money is equal. Evaluate investors on:
- **Reputation** — will their brand attract talent and future investors?
- **Value-add** — do they actually help, or just write checks?
- **Alignment** — do they share your vision for the company?
- **Follow-on capacity** — can they invest in future rounds?
- **References** — talk to founders they've backed, especially ones whose companies struggled

## 7. The Exit — How Everyone Gets Paid

VC-backed companies must eventually have a **liquidity event** — a moment when equity becomes cash.

### IPO (Initial Public Offering)

The company sells shares on a public stock exchange.

- **Pros:** Large amount of capital, prestige, liquidity for all shareholders, public currency (stock) for acquisitions and compensation
- **Cons:** Expensive ($5-20M in fees), ongoing compliance costs (SOX, SEC reporting), quarterly pressure, loss of privacy

### Acquisition

Another company buys yours.

- **Pros:** Faster than IPO, certain (if deal closes), can be lucrative
- **Cons:** Loss of independence, integration challenges, potential earn-out clauses

### Secondary sales

Shareholders sell their shares privately before an IPO. Common at late-stage companies. Provides partial liquidity.

### The liquidation preference waterfall

When a company is sold, money flows in a specific order:

```
1. Debt holders (banks, note holders)
2. Preferred shareholders (investors, by seniority)
3. Common shareholders (founders, employees)
```

Example: Company sells for $30M.
- Series A invested $10M with 1x liquidation preference
- Series A gets $10M first
- Remaining $20M split among all shareholders (or Series A converts to common and takes their pro-rata share — whichever is better)

In a *bad* exit (company sells for less than total invested), preferred shareholders get paid first. Common shareholders (founders, employees) may get nothing. This is why liquidation preferences matter.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Raising money is a tool, not an achievement.** It's fuel. Fuel without direction is waste.
2. **Understand VC incentives.** They need massive outcomes. Your good-but-not-great exit may not excite them.
3. **Dilution is normal.** 20% of something big > 100% of something small. But manage it carefully.
4. **Terms matter more than valuation.** A clean $15M valuation beats a messy $25M valuation.
5. **Not all money is equal.** Choose investors who add value beyond capital.
6. **SAFEs are standard for early rounds.** Simple, founder-friendly, no maturity date.
7. **Know how the waterfall works.** Liquidation preferences determine who gets paid in an exit.
