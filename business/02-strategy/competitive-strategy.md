# Competitive Strategy — Why Some Businesses Win

> Strategy is the art of making yourself hard to compete with. It's not about being
> the best — it's about being the *only*. Every company that survives long-term has
> figured out something about its competitive position that others can't easily
> replicate.

**Prerequisites:** [Economics](../01-foundations/economics.md) — You need to understand markets, competition, and incentives.
**What it enables:** [Business Models](business-models.md), [Product](../03-customers/product.md), [Growth](../06-growth/growth-and-scaling.md) — strategy shapes every downstream decision.

---

## Why This Matters

Most businesses fail not because they build bad products, but because they build good products in bad competitive positions. They enter crowded markets, compete on price, and grind their margins to zero.

Strategy is the antidote. It's the discipline of choosing *where* to play and *how* to win — and, critically, *where not to play*.

## 1. Porter's Five Forces — The Structure of an Industry

Michael Porter's framework is 45 years old and still the best starting point for understanding any industry. Five forces determine how much profit is available and who captures it:

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants

How easy is it for someone new to enter your market?

| Low threat (good for you) | High threat (bad for you) |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| High capital requirements | Low capital requirements |
| Strong brand loyalty | No brand loyalty |
| Regulatory barriers | No regulation |
| Network effects | No network effects |
| Proprietary technology | Commoditized technology |

If anyone with a laptop can build what you build, your margins will be competed away.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Suppliers

How much leverage do your suppliers have?

- Few suppliers = high power (they can raise prices)
- Many suppliers = low power (you can switch)
- If the supplier's product is differentiated or switching costs are high, they have power

Example: Apple has massive power over its suppliers because Apple is such a large buyer. Intel had massive power over PC makers because there was no alternative for x86 chips — until AMD caught up.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Buyers

How much leverage do your customers have?

- Few large customers = high power (they can demand discounts)
- Many small customers = low power (no single customer matters)
- Low switching costs = high power (they can leave easily)

Enterprise software often has high switching costs (your data is locked in, your team is trained on it), which reduces buyer power. Commodities have low switching costs — one barrel of oil is like any other.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes

Can customers solve the same problem in a completely different way?

- Video conferencing is a substitute for business travel
- Email is a substitute for postal mail
- Streaming is a substitute for cable TV

Substitutes cap the price you can charge. If your price gets too high, customers switch to the alternative.

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

How fiercely do existing players compete?

- Many competitors of similar size = intense rivalry
- Slow industry growth = everyone fights for share
- High fixed costs = pressure to fill capacity (airlines)
- Low differentiation = competition on price

```
                    Threat of
                   New Entrants
                       │
                       ▼
Bargaining Power ──► RIVALRY ◄── Bargaining Power
  of Suppliers    (among existing     of Buyers
                   competitors)
                       ▲
                       │
                   Threat of
                   Substitutes
```

### Using the Five Forces

The goal isn't to list the forces — it's to find an industry position where the forces are *favorable*:

- Barriers to entry keep competitors out
- Suppliers and buyers have limited power
- Substitutes are inferior
- Rivalry is manageable

If all five forces are strong, the industry is brutal (airlines, restaurants). If they're weak, the industry is attractive (enterprise SaaS with switching costs, regulated industries with licenses).

## 2. Competitive Advantage — The Two Routes

Porter identified two fundamental ways to win:

### Cost advantage
Do the same thing as competitors, but *cheaper*. Outcompete on price while maintaining margins.

How: economies of scale, process optimization, cheaper inputs, superior technology.

Examples: Walmart (logistics and scale), Ryanair (ruthless cost cutting), Amazon (infrastructure scale).

Warning: cost advantage is hard to sustain. Someone can always try to be cheaper. It works best at massive scale.

### Differentiation advantage
Do something competitors *can't* or *don't* — and charge more for it.

How: superior product, brand, design, service, network effects, unique technology.

Examples: Apple (design + ecosystem), Tesla (early EV brand), Salesforce (platform + ecosystem).

Differentiation is more defensible than cost advantage because it's harder to replicate. But it requires genuinely offering something different, not just marketing differently.

### Stuck in the middle

The worst position: you're not the cheapest, and you're not the most differentiated. You compete with everyone and have no clear advantage. Most failed businesses are stuck in the middle.

## 3. Moats — Durable Competitive Advantages

Warren Buffett's term for a sustainable competitive advantage that protects a business from competition, like a moat protects a castle.

### Types of moats

| Moat | How it works | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Network effects** | Product gets more valuable as more people use it | Facebook, Uber, Airbnb, Visa |
| **Switching costs** | It's painful to leave | Salesforce, Microsoft Office, banking |
| **Economies of scale** | Larger scale = lower unit costs | Walmart, Amazon, TSMC |
| **Brand** | Trust and recognition that takes years to build | Coca-Cola, Nike, Apple |
| **Regulatory / licenses** | Government limits competition | Telecom, banking, pharmaceuticals |
| **Proprietary technology** | Secret or patented tech | Google's search algorithm, ASML's EUV lithography |
| **Data advantages** | More data = better product = more data | Google, Waze, Netflix recommendations |
| **Counter-positioning** | Incumbents *can't* copy you without destroying their existing business | Netflix vs. Blockbuster, Tesla vs. dealership model |

### Network effects — the most powerful moat

A **network effect** exists when each new user makes the product more valuable for all existing users.

Types:
- **Direct** — each user benefits from more users (phone network, social media)
- **Indirect / platform** — more users attract more complementary providers (iOS: more users → more app developers → more users)
- **Data** — more users generate more data → better product (Google Search, Waze)
- **Marketplace** — more buyers attract more sellers and vice versa (eBay, Airbnb)

Network effects create winner-take-all (or winner-take-most) dynamics. Once you have the network, competitors can't just build a better product — they have to build a better product *and* recreate the network. This is extremely hard.

### The moat test

Ask yourself:
1. If a well-funded competitor built the same product tomorrow, would customers switch?
2. If yes — you don't have a moat.
3. If no — why not? That reason is your moat.

## 4. Disruption — How Incumbents Die

Clayton Christensen's theory of disruptive innovation explains why market leaders repeatedly get overthrown by smaller, weaker competitors.

### The pattern

1. An incumbent serves the mainstream market with an increasingly sophisticated product
2. A newcomer enters at the bottom — worse product, lower price, serves a market the incumbent ignores
3. The newcomer improves over time, eventually becoming "good enough" for the mainstream
4. By the time the incumbent reacts, it's too late

```
Performance
│              Incumbent's trajectory
│             ╱  (overshooting market needs)
│           ╱
│    ─────╱────── What the mainstream market needs
│       ╱
│     ╱   Disruptor's trajectory
│   ╱    (improving, crossing the line)
│ ╱
└──────────────── Time
```

### Why incumbents can't respond

It's not that they're stupid. It's that the rational response is to *not* respond:

- The disruptive market is small and low-margin (at first)
- Their best customers don't want the inferior product
- Cannibalizing their own high-margin product is irrational
- Their organizational structure is optimized for the existing market

This is the **innovator's dilemma** — doing the rational thing kills you.

### Examples

| Disruptor | Incumbent | What happened |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Netflix (streaming) | Blockbuster | Started with DVDs by mail (worse selection), then streaming |
| AWS | Traditional hosting | Started as "cheap, basic" cloud — now dominates enterprise |
| iPhone | Nokia/BlackBerry | Started as a "toy" — no keyboard, bad battery |
| Airbnb | Hotels | Started as "sleep on someone's air mattress" |
| Transistor | Vacuum tube | Started as inferior, became ubiquitous |

## 5. Strategic Positioning

Strategy isn't just about being better — it's about being *different*. Positioning is choosing a specific place in the market that you can own.

### Positioning dimensions

- **Customer segment** — who you serve (enterprise vs. SMB vs. consumer)
- **Problem** — which problem you solve (speed vs. cost vs. quality)
- **Price point** — premium vs. mid-market vs. budget
- **Geography** — local vs. national vs. global
- **Channel** — direct vs. indirect vs. platform

### Strategic trade-offs

Good strategy requires saying no. If you try to serve everyone, you serve no one well.

| Company | What they chose | What they gave up |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| In-N-Out | Simple menu, quality | Menu variety, speed of expansion |
| Rolex | Luxury positioning | Accessibility, volume |
| Southwest Airlines | Low cost, point-to-point | Business class, hub connectivity |
| Basecamp | Simplicity, small teams | Enterprise features, growth at all costs |

The choices you make *against* — the things you deliberately don't do — define your strategy as much as the things you do.

## 6. First-Mover vs. Fast-Follower

### First-mover advantage

Being first can help when:
- There are strong network effects (the first network wins)
- There are high switching costs (first entrants lock in customers)
- You can lock up key resources (patents, talent, distribution)
- Brand awareness matters (being synonymous with the category)

### Fast-follower advantage

Being second can help when:
- The first mover educates the market (you skip that cost)
- The first mover makes mistakes you can learn from
- Technology changes fast (first mover's tech becomes obsolete)
- The market isn't winner-take-all

| First mover won | Fast follower won |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Google (search, after others) | Facebook (after MySpace, Friendster) |
| Amazon (e-commerce) | Google (search — Yahoo, AltaVista were first) |
| iPhone (smartphone as we know it) | Samsung (fast-followed iPhone's form factor) |

The lesson: first-mover advantage is real but overrated. Timing matters more than order. Execution matters more than timing.

## 7. Blue Ocean vs. Red Ocean

**Red ocean:** Existing market. Known competitors. Competition is bloody (hence "red").
**Blue ocean:** New market you create. No direct competitors. Uncontested space.

| Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|-----------|
| Compete in existing market | Create new market space |
| Beat the competition | Make the competition irrelevant |
| Exploit existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| Value-cost trade-off | Break the value-cost trade-off |

Examples of blue oceans:
- Cirque du Soleil (circus + theater — neither traditional circus nor traditional theater)
- Nintendo Wii (casual gaming — not competing with PlayStation on graphics)
- Uber (not a better taxi — a new category)

Blue ocean strategy isn't about inventing new technology. It's about recombining existing elements in a way that creates a new value proposition for customers who weren't being served.

## 8. Strategy in Practice

### The strategy stack

A complete strategy answers these questions in order:

1. **Mission** — Why does this company exist?
2. **Vision** — What does the world look like if we succeed?
3. **Values** — What principles guide our decisions?
4. **Strategic position** — Where do we play? How do we win?
5. **Objectives** — What specific outcomes do we target?
6. **Tactics** — What actions do we take this quarter?

Each level constrains the one below it. Tactics without strategy is noise. Strategy without tactics is a dream.

### Strategy is about saying no

The biggest strategic mistakes come from trying to do too much:
- Entering markets you shouldn't be in
- Serving customers you can't serve well
- Adding features that dilute your positioning
- Chasing growth that destroys your moat

Jeff Bezos: "There are two kinds of companies — those that work to raise prices and those that work to lower them." Amazon chose. That choice (low prices, long-term thinking, customer obsession) eliminated entire categories of decisions.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Five forces determine industry profitability.** Choose industries where the forces are favorable.
2. **Win through cost or differentiation.** Never get stuck in the middle.
3. **Moats protect profits.** Network effects, switching costs, scale, and brand are the strongest.
4. **Disruption comes from below.** Incumbents die by ignoring the low end.
5. **Strategy is what you choose NOT to do.** Trade-offs are the essence of positioning.
6. **First-mover advantage is overrated.** Timing and execution matter more.
7. **Create blue oceans when you can.** The best competition is no competition.
