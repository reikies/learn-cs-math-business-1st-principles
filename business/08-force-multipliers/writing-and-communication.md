# Writing & Communication — The Universal Leverage

> Every great product needs great writing. Prompts, documentation, landing pages,
> emails, proposals, pitch decks, Slack messages, error messages, onboarding flows.
> Writing is the medium through which every other business skill is delivered.
> If you can't write clearly, you can't sell, you can't market, you can't lead,
> you can't fundraise, and you can't build products people understand.

**Prerequisites:** None. This is a force multiplier — it makes everything else more effective.
**What it multiplies:** [Marketing](../03-customers/marketing.md), [Sales](../03-customers/sales.md), [Product](../03-customers/product.md), [Fundraising](../05-finance/fundraising.md), [People](../04-operations/people-and-organizations.md) — every discipline that requires persuasion or clarity.

---

## Why This Matters

Writing is the most underrated skill in technology. Engineers who write well ship better products, get promoted faster, raise more money, and build bigger audiences. Founders who write well close more deals, attract better talent, and communicate vision more effectively.

This isn't about grammar. It's about thinking clearly enough to explain complex ideas simply — and persuasively enough to make people act.

In the AI era, this matters more, not less. You're writing prompts that steer AI behavior. You're writing documentation that explains AI products to non-technical buyers. You're writing landing pages that make "AI-powered" mean something specific instead of being empty buzzwords.

## 1. Clear Writing = Clear Thinking

Writing is not the output of thinking. Writing *is* thinking. If you can't explain something in writing, you don't understand it well enough.

### The clarity test

Take any idea you're working on and try to explain it in one paragraph to someone who knows nothing about your domain. If you can't:
- The idea is muddled
- You're hiding behind jargon
- You haven't identified what actually matters

### The principles of clear writing

| Principle | What it means | Example (bad → good) |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------|
| Short sentences | One idea per sentence | "Our platform leverages AI-driven analytics to provide actionable insights that enable cross-functional teams to optimize their workflows" → "Our tool shows teams where they're wasting time." |
| Active voice | Subject does the action | "The report was generated by the system" → "The system generated the report." |
| Concrete > abstract | Specific details over vague claims | "Significant improvements" → "40% faster response time" |
| Cut ruthlessly | Every word must earn its place | "In order to" → "To". "At this point in time" → "Now". "Due to the fact that" → "Because". |
| Front-load the point | Lead with the conclusion | Don't build up to the point. State it, then support it. |

### The Hemingway test

After writing anything, ask:
1. Can I cut this sentence? (If removing it changes nothing, cut it.)
2. Can I cut this word? (Adverbs are usually the first to go.)
3. Would a smart 14-year-old understand this? (If not, simplify.)

## 2. Writing for Different Business Contexts

### Product writing

The words inside your product *are* the product for many users.

| Element | What good looks like | What bad looks like |
|---------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Error messages | "Your file is too large (max 10MB). Try compressing it." | "Error: UPLOAD_FAILED_413" |
| Onboarding | "Send your first message" (one clear action) | "Welcome! Here are 15 features..." |
| Empty states | "No projects yet. Create one to get started." | Blank screen |
| Button labels | "Save changes" / "Delete project" (verb + noun) | "Submit" / "OK" / "Proceed" |
| Tooltips | One sentence explaining why, not what | A paragraph of documentation |

Product writing is UX design with words. Every screen, every button, every notification is a writing problem.

### Technical writing for non-technical audiences

This is how you sell AI to businesses. The skill: translating what something *does* (technically) into what it *means* (for them).

```
Technical:     "Our model uses retrieval-augmented generation with 
                a vector database to ground responses in your corpus."

Non-technical: "It reads your company's documents and answers questions 
                using only your information — not the internet."

Business:      "Your support team answers tickets 60% faster because 
                the AI drafts responses from your knowledge base."
```

The ladder: **How it works → What it does → Why you should care**

Always climb to the top rung. The business audience lives there.

### Persuasive writing (marketing, sales, fundraising)

Persuasion is not manipulation. It's making the reader's self-interest clear.

**The AIDA framework:**

```
Attention  → Hook them. ("Your support team is drowning in tickets.")
Interest   → Show you understand. ("Every ticket takes 8 minutes. 
              200 tickets/day = 27 hours of human time.")
Desire     → Paint the future. ("What if AI handled the routine ones 
              and your team only touched the complex cases?")
Action     → Make it easy. ("Start a free trial. No credit card.")
```

**The PAS framework (Problem-Agitate-Solve):**

```
Problem:   "Enterprise documentation is scattered across 40 tools."
Agitate:   "New hires take 3 months to find what they need. 
            Engineers waste 30% of their time searching."
Solve:     "Our AI indexes everything and answers in seconds."
```

### Email writing

Email is the most common business writing. Most emails are too long.

**The rules:**
1. **Subject line = the action you want.** "Need your approval on pricing by Friday" not "Quick question"
2. **First sentence = the point.** Don't bury the ask.
3. **5 sentences max for most emails.** If it takes more, it should be a document.
4. **One email = one ask.** Multiple requests get ignored.
5. **Make the action obvious.** Bold it. Put it at the end. Make it impossible to miss.

### Pitch writing (investors, partners, customers)

The structure that works:

```
1. The problem    (painful, specific, quantified)
2. The insight    (why existing solutions fail)
3. The solution   (what you built, briefly)
4. The proof      (traction, metrics, testimonials)
5. The market     (how big this can get)
6. The team       (why you're the ones to do it)
7. The ask        (what you want from the reader)
```

The most common mistake: spending 80% of the pitch on the solution and 5% on the problem. Flip it. If the audience doesn't feel the problem, they won't care about the solution.

## 3. Writing as a Growth Channel

Writing isn't just communication — it's distribution. (See [Marketing & Distribution](../03-customers/marketing-and-distribution.md).)

### Content marketing = writing that compounds

A blog post that ranks for a keyword drives traffic for years. A tweet disappears in hours. The compounding nature of written content is why content marketing is the most durable growth channel.

### Building in public

Writing about what you're building, learning, and struggling with creates:
- **Trust** — people see the thinking behind the product
- **Audience** — your writing attracts people who care about the same problems
- **Feedback** — public writing invites public reactions
- **Hiring pipeline** — talented people want to work with clear thinkers

### Documentation as marketing

For developer tools and technical products, documentation *is* the marketing. Stripe's API docs didn't just explain the product — they sold it. Developers evaluate tools by reading docs first.

Good documentation:
- Gets someone to "hello world" in under 5 minutes
- Explains concepts, not just API endpoints
- Has real, copy-pasteable code examples
- Anticipates the questions you'll have next

## 4. Communication Beyond Writing

### Presentations

The rules are the same as writing, but with fewer words per slide:
- **One idea per slide**
- **Speak the details, show the visuals** — don't read your slides
- **Story structure** — problem → journey → resolution
- **10/20/30 rule** (Guy Kawasaki): 10 slides, 20 minutes, 30pt font minimum

### Difficult conversations

The conversations that matter most — firing someone, giving hard feedback, negotiating a term sheet — require a specific kind of communication:

1. **State facts, not interpretations.** "You missed the last three deadlines" not "You're unreliable."
2. **Own your perspective.** "I'm concerned about..." not "Everyone thinks..."
3. **Be direct.** Don't soften the message until it disappears.
4. **Listen.** The goal is understanding, not winning.

### Writing prompts for AI

Prompt engineering is a writing discipline. The same principles apply:
- **Be specific.** Vague prompts → vague outputs.
- **Provide context.** What role, what audience, what constraints.
- **Show examples.** Few-shot prompting is "show, don't tell" applied to AI.
- **Iterate.** First prompts are first drafts. Refine.

## 5. How Writing Multiplies Everything Else

| Discipline | How writing multiplies it |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Marketing | Landing pages, blog posts, email campaigns, social media — it's all writing |
| Sales | Proposals, follow-up emails, case studies, objection handling scripts |
| Product | Feature specs, user stories, release notes, in-app copy, error messages |
| Fundraising | Pitch decks, investor memos, monthly updates to investors |
| Hiring | Job descriptions, employer brand content, offer letters |
| Leadership | Vision docs, strategy memos, all-hands presentations |
| AI products | Prompt engineering, documentation, explainability, trust-building |

Writing is the one skill that touches every other skill. Investing in it pays compound returns across your entire career.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Clear writing is clear thinking.** If you can't write it simply, you don't understand it.
2. **Every business context has its own writing rules.** Product copy, marketing, sales, technical docs — learn the conventions of each.
3. **Writing compounds.** Content is the only marketing channel that gets better over time.
4. **Translate up the ladder.** How it works → what it does → why you should care. The audience lives at the top.
5. **Cut ruthlessly.** The best writing is the shortest version that still communicates everything.
6. **Prompts are writing.** AI amplifies the difference between clear and vague communication.
