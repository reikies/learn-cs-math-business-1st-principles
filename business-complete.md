# Business & Entrepreneurship — The Complete Reference

> A single-file distillation of everything in [business/](business/). The principles
> apply whether you're selling Tom Ford cologne, a $99 Notion course, a SaaS product,
> a DTC skincare line, an enterprise AI platform, or a consultancy. The core
> mechanics of demand, distribution, and economics are universal. The tactics shift
> with the product type.

# LAYER 1 — FOUNDATIONS

## Economics

**The core idea:** Markets allocate scarce resources through prices. Every business decision is an economic decision.

### The essentials
- **Supply & demand** — Price rises → supply rises, demand falls. Equilibrium is where they meet.
- **Elasticity** — How sensitive quantity is to price. Inelastic (luxury, life-saving drugs) vs. elastic (commodities).
- **Marginal thinking** — Decisions happen at the margin. Cost/benefit of one more unit.
- **Opportunity cost** — The value of what you give up.
- **Comparative advantage** — Specialize in your highest relative strength, trade for the rest.
- **Market failures** — Externalities, public goods, information asymmetry, monopolies.
- **Game theory** — Strategic interaction. Why competitors converge on similar prices.

### Product-type lens
- **Luxury (Tom Ford):** Inelastic demand — higher price can *increase* perceived value (Veblen goods)
- **Commodities (soap, detergent):** Elastic demand — the cheapest wins unless you've built a brand
- **Marketplaces (Uber):** Network effects + cross-side subsidies (subsidize drivers to attract riders)
- **Info products (courses):** Zero marginal cost — scale is almost pure margin

### Key insight
Incentives drive behavior. To understand any system, look at what it rewards.

## Accounting & Financial Literacy

**The core idea:** Accounting is the language of business. Can't manage what you can't measure.

### The three statements

| Statement | What it shows | The question |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Balance sheet** | Assets = Liabilities + Equity at a point in time | What do we own and owe? |
| **Income statement (P&L)** | Revenue − Expenses = Profit over a period | Are we making money? |
| **Cash flow statement** | Cash in − Cash out over a period | Are we running out of cash? |

### Accrual vs. cash
- **Cash accounting** — Record when cash moves. Simple, misleading.
- **Accrual accounting** — Record when earned/incurred. Accurate. Required for real businesses.

**Critical distinction:** Profit ≠ cash. Profitable companies go bankrupt from cash problems all the time — especially physical product businesses with inventory cycles.

### Key concepts
- **Depreciation** — Spreading long-lived asset cost over useful life
- **Working capital** — Current assets − current liabilities. The cash cycle.
- **Gross margin** — Revenue minus direct costs (COGS)
- **EBITDA** — Approximates operating cash flow

### Product-type lens
- **Physical products (CPG, DTC):** Inventory on balance sheet ties up cash. Payment terms with retailers (Net 60, Net 90) can kill cash flow even when profitable.
- **SaaS:** Cash collected upfront (annual contracts), revenue recognized monthly. Deferred revenue is a *liability* but it's great.
- **Info products:** Near-zero COGS. Gross margin 90%+ once built.
- **Services:** Revenue recognized as work is done. Utilization rate drives profit.

## Legal Foundations

**The core idea:** The legal system defines what you can and can't do. Ignorance isn't a defense.

### Business entities

| Entity | Liability | Taxation | Use when |
|--------|-----------|----------|----------|
| Sole prop | Unlimited personal | Pass-through | Solo, low-risk |
| LLC | Limited | Pass-through (usually) | Most small businesses |
| C-Corp | Limited | Double (corp + dividends) | VC-funded, employee stock |
| S-Corp | Limited | Pass-through | Small US businesses wanting limited liability |

### Contracts
Offer + Acceptance + Consideration + Mutual intent. Key clauses: indemnification, limitation of liability, termination, IP ownership, confidentiality, dispute resolution.

### Intellectual property

| Type | Protects | Duration |
|------|----------|---------|
| Copyright | Creative works | Life + 70 years |
| Patent | Inventions | 20 years |
| Trademark | Brand identifiers | Indefinite (with use) |
| Trade secret | Confidential info | Indefinite (while secret) |

### Product-type lens
- **Luxury/fashion:** Trademarks and trade dress are existential (counterfeit war)
- **Physical products:** Product liability insurance, FDA/CPSC regulations
- **DTC / e-commerce:** Consumer protection laws, return policies, FTC rules on advertising
- **Info products:** Copyright on content, terms of service limiting sharing
- **SaaS:** Data protection (GDPR, CCPA), SLAs, customer data ownership
- **Franchises:** FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document), state registration

# LAYER 2 — STRATEGY

## Competitive Strategy

### Porter's Five Forces
1. Threat of new entrants
2. Bargaining power of suppliers
3. Bargaining power of buyers
4. Threat of substitutes
5. Rivalry among existing competitors

### The three generic strategies
- **Cost leadership** — Win by being cheapest (Walmart, Ryanair, Costco)
- **Differentiation** — Win by being unique (Apple, Tom Ford, Tesla)
- **Focus** — Dominate a niche (Ferrari, Supreme, in-flight WiFi specialists)

### Moats (durable competitive advantages)
- **Network effects** — More users = more value (Facebook, Airbnb, eBay)
- **Switching costs** — Painful to leave (Salesforce, enterprise software, Apple ecosystem)
- **Economies of scale** — Lower cost per unit (Amazon, Walmart, P&G)
- **Brand** — Trust/identity that commands premium (Coca-Cola, Hermès, Disney)
- **Intangible assets** — Patents, licenses, regulatory barriers (pharma, utilities)
- **Process power** — Unique operational capability (Toyota, In-N-Out)
- **Cornered resource** — Exclusive access (rare minerals, star talent)

### Product-type lens
- **Luxury CPG (Tom Ford, Rolex):** Moat = brand mystique + controlled distribution + heritage. Can't be bought overnight.
- **DTC:** Moat = community + repeat purchase + customer data. Weak moat — easily copied unless brand transcends product.
- **SaaS:** Moat = switching costs + integrations + data accumulation
- **Marketplaces:** Moat = network effects (liquidity)
- **Info products:** Moat = creator identity + audience relationship
- **Commodity CPG:** Moat = distribution + scale + brand recognition

### Disruption (Christensen)
Incumbents optimize for their best customers. Disruptors serve underserved segments with "inferior" products that improve rapidly. Classic examples: Toyota vs. Detroit, Netflix vs. Blockbuster, DTC brands vs. legacy CPG.

## Business Models

**The core idea:** A great product with no business model is a hobby. The model determines whether you survive.

### Common revenue patterns

| Model | How it works | Best for |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **One-time sale** | Pay once, own it | Consumer goods, hardware, furniture |
| **Subscription** | Recurring payment | Media, software, consumables |
| **SaaS** | Recurring software access | B2B software |
| **Freemium** | Free core, paid upgrade | Software with viral loops |
| **Marketplace / take-rate** | % of each transaction | Two-sided markets |
| **Advertising** | Free to users, sell attention | Content, social platforms |
| **Transaction fee** | Per-transaction cut | Payments, commerce platforms |
| **Licensing** | Rent IP | Brands, patents, tech |
| **Franchise / royalty** | Operators pay % for brand + system | Scalable local businesses |
| **Usage-based** | Pay per unit consumed | Infrastructure, APIs |
| **Outcome-based** | Pay for results | Performance marketing, some AI |
| **Razor-and-blades** | Cheap base, profit on consumables | Printers, coffee pods, razors |

### Pricing strategies across product types

| Type | Common approach | Key principle |
|------|----------------|--------------|
| **Luxury CPG** | Premium fixed price | Never discount (destroys brand) |
| **Mass CPG** | Low price, promotion cycles | Price competes, brand retains |
| **DTC** | Mid-premium + bundles | Test price sensitivity via A/B |
| **SaaS** | Per-seat tiers + enterprise custom | Anchor high, create clear tier value gaps |
| **Info products** | Tiered ($X, $3X, $10X) | Price-quality inference — higher price = perceived higher value |
| **Services** | Hourly, project, or retainer | Value-based > hourly whenever possible |
| **Marketplaces** | % take-rate | Balance supplier margin against demand |

### Unit economics (universal)
- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost) — all acquisition spend ÷ new customers
- **LTV** (Lifetime Value) — avg revenue × gross margin × lifetime
- **LTV/CAC** — ≥ 3x for sustainable business
- **Payback period** — months to recoup CAC. < 12-18 months is healthy.
- **Contribution margin** — after variable costs, what's left to cover fixed costs?

### Product-specific unit economics gotchas
- **DTC:** Returns, shipping, and paid acquisition can make CAC higher than LTV for one-time purchases. Need repeat purchase to work.
- **SaaS:** Churn is the killer. 2% monthly churn = customers last ~4 years. 10% monthly churn = less than a year.
- **Marketplaces:** Have to get unit economics on *both* sides. Need enough supply and demand density.
- **Physical products:** COGS includes materials, labor, shipping, and returns — easy to underestimate.

# LAYER 3 — CUSTOMERS (THE TRINITY)

## Pillar 1: Product Thinking

**The core idea:** Product thinking is different from engineering or manufacturing. It asks "should this exist?" The #1 cause of business failure is "no market need."

### The mindset
- **Problems > solutions.** Fall in love with the problem, not your idea.
- **Behavior > opinions.** What people *do* beats what they *say*.
- **Speed > polish (early).** Ship fast, learn faster.
- **Reality > theory.** Real usage beats projected usage.

### User research hierarchy (most to least reliable)
1. **Observed behavior** — Watch people actually use/buy
2. **Past actions** — "Tell me about the last time you..."
3. **Feelings** — "How did that feel?"
4. **Opinions** — "What do you think of X?"
5. **Stated intent** — "Would you buy this?" (lies)

### The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
- Talk about their life, not your idea
- Ask about the past, not the future
- Listen more than you talk

### Jobs to Be Done
People don't buy products. They "hire" products to do a job.
- **Milkshake** competing with bagels for the morning commute
- **Rolex** competing with BMW for "I've made it" signaling
- **Peloton** competing with SoulCycle + gym membership for "fitness at home"
- **Course** competing with YouTube + books + boot camp for "level up my career"

### Product-Market Fit
**The Sean Ellis test:** "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" 40%+ "very disappointed" = PMF.

**Equivalents by product type:**
- **CPG/DTC:** Repeat purchase rate, organic reorder
- **Luxury:** Waitlists, resale premiums (Birkin bags trade above retail)
- **SaaS:** High retention, expansion revenue, organic signups
- **Info products:** High completion rates, unsolicited testimonials, referrals
- **Services:** Word-of-mouth > paid acquisition

### Prototyping fidelity ladder
Varies by product type but same idea — test before committing.

| Product type | Cheap prototype | Medium | Full |
|-------------|----------------|--------|------|
| Software | Figma mockup | Clickable prototype | Functional MVP |
| DTC / CPG | Dropshipped version | Small batch run | Full production |
| Info product | Live workshop | Cohort-based pilot | On-demand course |
| Service | Free consultations | Paid pilot engagements | Productized service |
| Hardware | 3D print | Small manufacturing run | Tooled mass production |

### MVP types (work for almost any product)
- **Landing page MVP** — Describe product, collect signups/pre-orders
- **Wizard of Oz** — Looks automated/polished, manually delivered behind the scenes
- **Concierge** — Hand-deliver the service to first 10 customers
- **Single-feature** / **single-SKU** — Build one thing only
- **Fake door** — Button for a feature/product that doesn't exist; measure clicks

### Prioritization
- **RICE:** (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
- **ICE:** Impact × Confidence × Ease
- **The one-question test:** "Does this move us toward (or deepen) PMF?" If no, it's a distraction.

### Shipping judgment — when to be rough vs. polished

| Rough is fine | Polished is required |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Validating demand | Onboarding first paying customer |
| Early B2B pilots | Luxury / premium positioning |
| Internal tools | Brand-trust-driven products (finance, beauty, health) |
| Software MVPs | Physical products you can't recall |
| Ugly landing pages that *convert* | Products where design IS the product (Apple, Dyson) |

**Universal rule:** For luxury and brand-driven products, polish is non-negotiable from day one. You don't get a second first impression.

### Common failure modes (by product type)

| Type | How products fail |
|------|------------------|
| Physical / CPG | Hero feature not valued; supply chain kills margins; can't scale production |
| DTC | Paid acquisition treadmill; no repeat purchase; thin margins |
| Luxury | Brand dilution through bad distribution or discounting |
| SaaS | Nice-to-have, not must-have; churn > acquisition |
| Info products | Creator builds for themselves, not audience; no delivery on promise |
| Marketplaces | Never reach liquidity on either side |
| Services | Founder-dependent; can't productize; doesn't scale |
| Franchises | Playbook not actually replicable by average operator |

## Pillar 2: Marketing & Distribution

**The core idea:** The best product in the world fails if nobody knows it exists. Distribution is the main quest.

### The distribution hierarchy (most durable → least durable)
1. **Built-in virality** — Product spreads through use (Figma, Zoom, WhatsApp)
2. **Owned audience** — Email list, SMS list, community you control
3. **Owned platform** — Your website, your content library
4. **Partnerships & integrations** — Borrow someone else's distribution
5. **Rented audience** — Social media followers (platform controls)
6. **Paid ads** — Works only while you pay
7. **PR** — One-time spikes, no compounding

### Positioning (April Dunford)
1. Competitive alternatives — what would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. Unique attributes — what do you have they don't?
3. Value — what does that uniqueness give the customer?
4. Target customers — who cares most?
5. Market category — what context makes your value obvious?

**Positioning is the same question across products:** Tom Ford positions *against* mass fragrance (Axe) and *as* sophisticated luxury. Warby Parker positioned *against* Luxottica's monopoly and *as* accessible, stylish eyewear. Notion positioned *against* Google Docs + Trello + Evernote and *as* the unified workspace.

### The marketing funnel (AARRR)
- **Acquisition** — how do people find you?
- **Activation** — do they have a good first experience?
- **Retention** — do they come back?
- **Revenue** — do they pay?
- **Referral** — do they tell others?

### Channel fit by product type

| Product type | Channels that usually work |
|-------------|----------------------------|
| **DTC / e-commerce** | Paid social (Meta, TikTok), influencers, SEO for product keywords, email/SMS |
| **Luxury CPG** | Press, curated stockists, celebrity/influencer placement, flagship stores |
| **Mass CPG** | TV + retail shelves, in-store displays, coupons, Amazon |
| **SaaS (SMB)** | Content/SEO, PLG + self-serve, LinkedIn ads, communities |
| **SaaS (enterprise)** | Outbound sales, events, partnerships, account-based marketing |
| **Info products** | Personal brand on social, email list, podcast, affiliates |
| **Services** | Referrals, content marketing, speaking, networking |
| **Marketplaces** | Supply-side first (manual), then demand-side (content + paid) |
| **Hardware** | Reviews, flagship retail, pre-order campaigns (Kickstarter) |

### Content marketing — the compounding channel
Works for almost every product type. A blog post that ranks drives traffic for years.

- **DTC:** "Best X for Y" guides, product comparisons, lifestyle content
- **SaaS:** Educational content on the problem space (HubSpot wrote the book on inbound)
- **Luxury:** Lookbooks, editorial, heritage stories
- **Info products:** Free content that demonstrates expertise (the product *is* the marketing)
- **Services:** Case studies, thought leadership, methodology breakdowns

### SEO essentials (apply to most product types)
- Keyword research — intent-rich terms with manageable competition
- On-page — title tag, H1, content quality, internal links
- Technical — speed, mobile, structured data
- Backlinks — authority from others linking to you

### Community building (4 phases)
1. **Gather** — First 100 true fans
2. **Cultivate** — Create rituals, norms, shared language
3. **Scale** — Moderation, structure, sub-groups
4. **Ecosystem** — Community becomes distribution

Works powerfully for: info products, developer tools, hobbyist CPG (skincare, coffee, wine), games, fitness brands.

### PLG vs. SLG (for software)

```
                    Simple product         Complex product
Low price (ACV)    Product-Led Growth     Usually doesn't work
High price (ACV)   PLG + Sales expansion  Sales-Led Growth
```

### Retail distribution (for physical products)
- **Direct (DTC):** Own the relationship, own the margin, own the data. But you pay for traffic.
- **Marketplace (Amazon):** Massive reach, you give up margin and data.
- **Wholesale / specialty retail:** Brand validation, but thin margins and slow cash.
- **Mass retail (Target, Walmart):** Scale and awareness; brutal margins and slotting fees.
- **Luxury / prestige (Saks, Bergdorf):** Signals positioning. Key for brand perception.

### Launch strategy patterns

| Product type | Classic launch pattern |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Software | Private beta → launch on Product Hunt / HN → wider release |
| DTC | Waitlist → founding customer launch → paid acquisition ramp |
| Luxury CPG | Editorial seeding → exclusive boutique → limited drop |
| Info product | Free content → email list → pre-launch sequence → cart open window |
| Hardware | Kickstarter/Indiegogo → press → retail |
| Services | Free/cheap pilots → case study → referral engine |

### Key metrics
- **CAC** — Customer Acquisition Cost
- **LTV / CAC ratio** — need ≥ 3x
- **ROAS** — Return on Ad Spend (target 3-5x for most DTC)
- **Conversion rate** — varies wildly (1-3% DTC cold traffic, 15-30% warm email)
- **Repeat purchase rate** — for DTC/CPG, > 30% within 90 days is healthy
- **AOV** (Average Order Value) — DTC lever: bundles, upsells
- **NPS** — Net Promoter Score

## Pillar 3: Sales & The Business Layer

### Sales

**The core idea:** Marketing creates interest. Sales converts interest into revenue.

**Sales model spectrum (matches price × complexity):**

```
Self-serve → Inside sales → Field sales → Strategic/Enterprise
($10-500)    ($500-5K)      ($5K-50K)      ($50K+)
```

Applies across product types:
- **DTC:** Self-serve (checkout)
- **SaaS SMB:** Self-serve or inside sales
- **Enterprise software:** Field / strategic sales
- **Luxury retail:** In-store consultative selling
- **High-end services:** Consultative selling with multiple stakeholders
- **Franchises:** B2B sales to potential franchisees

**BANT qualification:** Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline. Missing any → rarely closes.

**SPIN discovery** (for complex sales):
- **S**ituation, **P**roblem, **I**mplication, **N**eed-payoff

**Pricing principles:**
- Price on value, not cost
- Anchor high
- Tiered pricing captures more value
- Annual > monthly
- Never discount without getting something

**Luxury exception:** Never discount, period. Discounting destroys brand equity. Better to burn inventory (as some fashion houses do) than mark down.

**Enterprise sales:** Multiple decision-makers (end user, champion, economic buyer, technical evaluator, executive sponsor). 3-12 month cycles. Multi-thread or die.

**Key metrics:**
- Pipeline (3-4x target)
- Win rate (20-30% enterprise, 5-15% SMB)
- Sales cycle length
- Quota attainment (60-70% = calibrated)
- Net Revenue Retention (>100% = existing customers growing)

# LAYER 4 — OPERATIONS & PEOPLE

## Operations

**The core idea:** A business that depends on heroics doesn't scale. Operations turns chaos into repeatable systems.

### Core concepts
- **Process mapping** — document how work actually happens
- **Bottlenecks (Theory of Constraints)** — throughput of a system = throughput of its weakest link
- **Quality systems** — Six Sigma (defect reduction), Lean (waste elimination)
- **Supply chain** — sourcing, inventory, logistics, JIT vs. buffer stock
- **Toyota Production System** — Kaizen, pull systems, respect for people

### Product-type lens
- **Physical / CPG:** Operations *is* the business. Supply chain, manufacturing, QA, logistics, returns.
- **Luxury:** Hand-made at scale. Quality control is brand-critical. Artisanal positioning = slower, more expensive operations.
- **DTC:** 3PLs (third-party logistics), inventory forecasting, returns processing.
- **SaaS:** Infrastructure uptime, incident response, customer support.
- **Info products:** Content production pipeline, delivery tech, community management.
- **Services:** Utilization, project management, quality consistency across practitioners.
- **Franchises:** The *operations manual* IS the product you're selling to franchisees.

### Operational metrics
Throughput, cycle time, lead time, defect rate, capacity utilization, on-time delivery, fill rate.

## People & Organizations

### Hiring
- **Early stage:** hire for attitude, train for skills
- **Scaling stage:** hire for specific skills
- **Structured interviews** > gut feel
- **Reference checks** are underrated

### Culture
- Culture is what people do when no one's watching
- Culture comes from what leaders *tolerate*, not what they say
- Written values matter only when they cost you something

### Org design
- **Functional** (by skill) — efficient, good small/mid
- **Divisional** (by product/market) — flexibility, good for diversified companies
- **Matrix** (both) — flexibility + conflict
- **Flat** — fast, breaks at ~50 people

### Motivation (Self-Determination Theory)
Autonomy, mastery, purpose.

### Rule of three and ten (Mikitani)
Companies break at multiples of 3 and 10. Each breakpoint requires rebuilding processes and leadership.

### Product-type lens
- **Physical / CPG:** Operators, supply-chain ops, retail merchandisers dominate headcount. Low revenue-per-employee.
- **Luxury:** Artisans, retail staff, brand/creative leads. Hiring for taste and craft, not just skill.
- **DTC:** Lean — ops, performance marketing, creative, CX. Outsource fulfillment (3PL), insource brand.
- **SaaS:** Engineering + sales + CS. Revenue-per-employee is the scaling KPI ($200K+ at scale).
- **Info products / creator businesses:** Often solo or a tight team (ops, editor, CX). Founder is the brand.
- **Services:** Utilization is the org design constraint. Senior/junior pyramid determines margin.
- **Franchises:** HQ is small; the "organization" is thousands of independent operators you don't employ.

# LAYER 5 — FINANCE

## Corporate Finance

### Time value of money
A dollar today > a dollar tomorrow. PV = FV / (1+r)^n.

### Valuation methods
- **DCF** — discounted cash flows
- **Comparables** — revenue/EBITDA multiples of similar companies
- **Precedent transactions** — what similar companies sold for
- **Startup:** revenue multiples, ARR multiples, stage heuristics

### Capital structure
- **Debt** — cheap, must be repaid, bankruptcy risk
- **Equity** — expensive (investors want returns), no repayment obligation
- Optimal structure balances cost with risk

### Product-type lens
- **Physical / CPG:** Working capital is the main financial problem — inventory + receivables tie up cash. Debt (lines of credit, PO financing) is normal and healthy.
- **Luxury:** Long brand-build runway. Often funded by patient family capital or conglomerate balance sheets (LVMH, Kering).
- **DTC:** Cash conversion cycle is king — Shopify + Klaviyo dashboards matter more than DCF. Revenue-based financing common.
- **SaaS:** Collect cash upfront (annual), recognize monthly. Deferred revenue funds growth. Valued on ARR multiples.
- **Info products / creator:** Near-zero working capital. Profit drops to owner. Valuation is earnings multiple if sold at all.
- **Services / agencies:** Project billing + retainers. Valued on EBITDA multiples (3-8x). Roll-ups common.
- **Marketplaces:** Negative working capital at scale (hold payouts). Valued on GMV and take-rate multiples.

## Fundraising vs. Bootstrapping

### When to bootstrap
- Small or niche market
- Cash-flow positive possible early
- Founders value control
- Product doesn't require winner-take-all speed
- **Classic bootstrap domains:** info products, services, local businesses, premium DTC, small SaaS

### When to raise
- Winner-take-all market
- Speed is the constraint
- Heavy upfront capital needed (hardware, marketplaces)
- **Classic VC domains:** enterprise SaaS, marketplaces, AI platforms, fintech, deep tech

### Funding stages

| Stage | Size | Source | Milestone |
|-------|------|--------|-----------|
| Pre-seed | $100K-$1M | Angels, F&F | Idea + team |
| Seed | $1M-$5M | Seed funds | MVP, early traction |
| Series A | $5M-$20M | VCs | PMF, early revenue |
| Series B | $20M-$60M | VCs | Unit economics, scaling |
| Series C+ | $60M+ | Growth equity | Market expansion |

### Key instruments
- **SAFE** — most common early. No interest/maturity. Converts at next round.
- **Convertible note** — with interest and maturity
- **Priced round** — sets valuation; preferred stock with rights

### Term sheet essentials
- Pre-money vs. post-money valuation
- Liquidation preference (1x non-participating = founder-friendly)
- Pro-rata rights
- Anti-dilution
- Board composition
- Option pool (10-20%)

### Physical product financing
Inventory-heavy businesses can use:
- **Revenue-based financing** (Clearco, Wayflyer)
- **Inventory / purchase order financing**
- **Factoring** (sell receivables)
- **Traditional bank loans / lines of credit**

## Financial Modeling & Metrics

### The three core models
1. **Revenue** — unit economics + acquisition + retention
2. **Expense** — headcount, infrastructure, marketing, COGS
3. **Cash flow** — timing matters more than totals

### SaaS benchmarks
- ARR/MRR, gross margin > 70%, LTV/CAC ≥ 3x, payback < 12-18mo, NRR > 100%, Rule of 40 (growth% + margin ≥ 40%)

### DTC / e-commerce benchmarks
- Gross margin > 40% (>60% is great)
- Contribution margin after paid acquisition and shipping
- Repeat purchase rate > 30% within 90 days
- Blended CAC payback < 6 months ideally (you don't have recurring revenue)

### Luxury / premium CPG benchmarks
- Gross margin > 70%
- Inventory turns (not as critical as mass)
- Full-price sell-through > 70% (discounting erodes brand)

### Info products benchmarks
- Gross margin > 90%
- Email list size and engagement
- Conversion on launches (1-3% of email list is good)

### Services benchmarks
- Utilization rate 60-80%
- Revenue per employee
- Gross margin 40-60% (depends on leverage)

# LAYER 6 — GROWTH & SCALING

**The core idea:** 0 to 1 is invention. 1 to 100 is scaling. Different discipline entirely.

### Growth hierarchy (do in order)
1. PMF
2. Unit economics
3. Repeatable acquisition
4. Scale

### Growth loops (compound better than funnels)
- **Content loop** — content → SEO → traffic → users → more content
- **Viral loop** — user → invite → new user → invite
- **Paid loop** — ad spend → customer → revenue → more ad spend
- **Referral loop** — customer → referral reward → new customer
- **UGC loop** — user creates content → discoverable → more users (TikTok, YouTube, Pinterest)
- **Product loop** — users get value → use more → product gets better
- **Retail loop** — strong sell-through → more shelf space → more sales

### Retention — the silent growth driver
1,000/mo acquisition with 10% churn → plateaus at 10K. Same acquisition with 2% churn → 20K+ and growing.

### Retention by product type
- **SaaS:** 2% monthly churn is healthy; 10% is death spiral
- **DTC:** Repeat purchase rate — 30%+ in 90 days is strong; 60%+ is exceptional
- **Subscription boxes:** Cancellation rate < 5%/mo is healthy
- **Info products:** Course completion + repeat purchases across catalog
- **Services:** Account retention / contract renewal

### GTM motions

| Motion | Best for |
|--------|---------|
| Product-Led Growth | Simple software, low ACV |
| Sales-Led Growth | Complex software, high ACV |
| Marketing-Led Growth | Mid-market SaaS, DTC |
| Community-Led Growth | Developer tools, niche CPG, info products |
| Partnership-Led Growth | Ecosystems, retail, wholesale |
| Retail-Led Growth | Mass CPG, mass consumer |

### Network effects (strongest software moat)
Cold start: start niche (Facebook at Harvard), fake supply (Yelp, Reddit), subsidize one side (Uber drivers), create standalone value (Instagram as photo editor first).

### International expansion
Replicate, adapt, acquire, or partner. Translation is 10%; cultural/regulatory/operational adaptation is 90%.

### Product-type scaling challenges

| Type | The scaling wall |
|------|-----------------|
| Physical / CPG | Manufacturing capacity, supply chain, cash-tied-up in inventory |
| DTC | Rising CAC, channel saturation, margin compression |
| Luxury | Growing without diluting brand exclusivity |
| SaaS | Moving up-market (enterprise) without losing SMB velocity |
| Info products | Beyond founder-led; systematizing content and marketing |
| Services | Productize or hire at quality — both are hard |
| Marketplaces | Reaching liquidity in new geos; fighting off take-rate pressure |

# LAYER 7 — ENTREPRENEURSHIP

### Ideas vs. problems
First-time founders obsess over ideas. Experienced founders obsess over problems. Best ideas come from personal frustration, domain expertise, technological change, or market shifts.

### The stages (and what kills you at each)
1. **Ideation (0-3mo)** — building before validating
2. **MVP (1-3mo)** — over-building, perfectionism
3. **Finding PMF (3-18mo)** — running out of money, premature scaling
4. **Early growth (6-18mo)** — leaky bucket, hiring too fast
5. **Scaling (1-3yr)** — chaos, culture dilution
6. **Maturity (3-10yr)** — complacency, disruption by next gen

### Pivoting
- Signals: users don't come back, growth requires heroics, can't articulate why customers choose you
- A pivot preserves learning (team, relationships, insights)
- Commit fully — half-pivot is worst

### The founding team
- 2-3 founders (solo = higher failure rate)
- Complementary skills
- Prior relationship (trust under pressure)
- Full-time commitment

### Common traps
Founder mythology, sunk cost attachment, hero syndrome, comparison, burnout.

### Founder evolution
Starts: doing everything. Scales: hiring people to do everything. Matures: deciding what *not* to do. The skills that start a company are not the skills that scale it.

### Founder archetype by product type

| Type | What the founder must be great at |
|------|----------------------------------|
| Software / SaaS | Product judgment + recruiting engineers + selling to first customers |
| DTC / CPG | Brand taste + performance marketing + supply chain literacy |
| Luxury | Aesthetic conviction + patience + refusing to compromise for growth |
| Info products / creator | Writing/on-camera presence + audience building + teaching |
| Services | Sales + delivery excellence + hiring senior talent |
| Marketplaces | Cold-start hustle (manual supply) + two-sided marketing |
| Hardware | Engineering + manufacturing + capital orchestration |
| Franchise | Operations obsession + B2B selling to franchisees |

### Exit paths (vary wildly by type)
- **SaaS:** Strategic acquisition (most common), PE roll-up, rarely IPO
- **DTC / CPG:** Strategic acquirer (P&G, Unilever, L'Oréal pattern), aggregator (Thrasio-style), PE
- **Luxury:** Conglomerate acquisition (LVMH, Kering, Estée Lauder)
- **Info products / creator:** Rarely sold — cash-flow business held by founder
- **Services / agencies:** PE roll-up, merger, or founder takes dividends
- **Marketplaces:** IPO if winner; acquisition if #2
- **Hardware:** Strategic acquirer or IPO; often acquihire if failed

# FORCE MULTIPLIERS

## Writing & Communication

**The core idea:** Every business skill is delivered through writing. Clear writing is clear thinking.

### Principles
- Short sentences, active voice, concrete over abstract
- Cut ruthlessly — every word earns its place
- Front-load the point

### Where writing wins across product types
- **DTC:** Product page copy, email sequences, ad creative
- **Luxury:** Brand storytelling, heritage copy, editorial content
- **SaaS:** Documentation, onboarding emails, release notes
- **Info products:** Sales pages, email sequences, the content itself
- **Services:** Proposals, case studies, thought leadership content
- **All:** Pitch decks, investor updates, job descriptions

### Frameworks
- **AIDA** — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
- **PAS** — Problem, Agitate, Solve

---

## Data Literacy & Analytics

**The core idea:** Reading charts, understanding distributions, knowing which metrics matter.

### Universal principles
- Averages lie; look at distributions and cohorts
- Vanity metrics feel good, actionable metrics drive decisions
- Cohort analysis is the most important technique
- SQL fluency = answer your own questions

### Product-specific metrics to master

| Type | Metrics that matter most |
|------|-------------------------|
| DTC / e-commerce | AOV, repeat rate, ROAS, contribution margin |
| Luxury | Full-price sell-through, inventory turns, brand health |
| SaaS | MRR, NRR, churn, CAC payback, magic number |
| Info products | Email list size, launch conversion, completion rates |
| Marketplaces | Take-rate, GMV, liquidity, repeat rate |
| Services | Utilization, revenue per employee, project margin |

### Data storytelling
Context → Insight → Implication → Action. Raw data doesn't convince; stories do.

---

## Design (Basic UI/UX + Brand)

**The core idea:** You don't need to be a designer. You need enough taste to not build ugly, confusing things.

### Information hierarchy (the #1 skill)
Size, weight, color, spacing, position. The squint test.

### Spacing & alignment
8px base unit, consistent multiples. Most "amateur" designs fail at spacing, not color.

### Typography
One font family. 16px+ body. Line height 1.5. 45-75 chars per line.

### Color
60/30/10 rule. Gray + one accent covers 90% of cases.

### Design by product type
- **Luxury CPG:** Design IS the product. Packaging, unboxing, brand system.
- **DTC:** Design drives conversion. Hero imagery, lifestyle content, typography.
- **Software:** Information hierarchy, user flows, progressive disclosure.
- **Info products:** Course UX + brand consistency across touchpoints.
- **Services:** Deck design, proposal design — signals competence.

### Packaging for physical products
For DTC and luxury, the packaging is the first physical interaction. It is marketing, brand, and product in one. Underinvestment here is a common failure.

## Psychology & Behavioral Science

**The core idea:** People are predictably irrational. Marketing, UX, sales, and negotiation are all applied psychology.

### Biases that matter for builders
- **Anchoring** — first number sets the frame (pricing)
- **Loss aversion** — losses hurt 2x more than equivalent gains
- **Social proof** — reviews, testimonials, "best-seller" tags
- **Status quo bias** — "do nothing" is your biggest competitor
- **Sunk cost** — why customers stick with bad products
- **Framing** — same fact, different reaction

### System 1 vs. System 2
- System 1 (fast, emotional): landing pages, retail shelves, impulse buys
- System 2 (slow, analytical): enterprise sales, large purchases, B2B RFPs

### Product-type psychology

| Type | Dominant psychology |
|------|--------------------|
| Luxury | Status, scarcity, identity, Veblen effect |
| DTC | Social proof, FOMO, free shipping threshold, personalization |
| SaaS | Loss aversion (switching costs), social proof (logos), peer adoption |
| Info products | Authority (credentials), social proof (transformations), scarcity (cart close) |
| Services | Trust equation, reciprocity, liking |
| Subscription | Pain of paying (hidden), habit formation |

### Pricing psychology
Anchoring, charm pricing ($9.99 — avoid for luxury), price-quality inference, decoy effect, the power of free.

### Cialdini's six principles
Reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity.

### Ethics test
"Would the customer thank me for this design choice if they understood it?" Yes = alignment. No = manipulation.

# UNIVERSAL KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Problems > solutions.** Start with what's painful, not what's cool.
2. **Product-market fit is the only thing that matters** until you have it.
3. **Distribution is the main quest.** Great product + no distribution = failure. Mediocre product + great distribution = often wins.
4. **Unit economics don't lie.** LTV/CAC > 3 or the business isn't real.
5. **Retention > acquisition.** A leaky bucket can't be filled.
6. **Moats are earned.** Network effects, switching costs, scale, brand — build one or you're commoditized.
7. **Cash > profit.** Profitable companies go bankrupt from cash flow problems.
8. **The team is the company.** Early hires shape culture forever.
9. **Founders must evolve.** The skills that start a company are not the skills that scale it.
10. **Match product type to tactics.** The principles are universal; the playbook differs. Don't run SaaS plays on a luxury brand.
11. **Force multipliers compound.** Writing, data, design, and psychology make every other skill more effective.
